## Claim

The remote/office decision matters more than founders typically acknowledge because it directly determines who you can hire, how onboarding works, and what cultural habits form — all of which are very difficult to change once set.

## Source quote or paraphrase

HN thread on remote vs. in-person: "For me, I'd go 100% remote from the start. It's better to build that into the DNA of the company from the start." Counter-view: "For very early stage (2-5 people), in-person makes sense — you're building the business together, making tons of moment-by-moment changes, and the working relationship is critical." The key agreement across both sides: the decision shapes the DNA, and retrofitting it is painful.

## Why (inferred from source if not explicit)

The norms and expectations that form with the first 3–5 people become the baseline that every subsequent hire is calibrated against. An implicit office culture — where people drift into coming in daily without a policy saying so — will produce resentment when employee #6 joins expecting the flexibility they were told existed. An implicit remote culture — where nobody ever documents or communicates explicitly because "we all know" — will break at the first hire who is not already inside the founding team's mental model. The mechanism is not just about location; it is about what habits of communication and documentation the team develops in its first 6 months.
