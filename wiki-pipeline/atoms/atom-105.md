## Claim

Make the remote/office decision explicit before or with the first hire — write it down and share it. The default ("we'll see how it goes") is itself a decision, and it tends to produce the worst outcome.

## Source quote or paraphrase

Multiple HN practitioners note that the decision should match the team's circumstances, communication style, and co-founder geography — and that it should be made consciously, not by inertia. "It does not have to be black and white — start remote, but local to each other to get together as needed" is cited as a viable middle path. The key is that it is a stated policy, not an implicit assumption.

## Why (inferred from source if not explicit)

The remote/office policy determines who you can hire (local vs. global), how new hires calibrate their expectations, and what cultural habits form in the first 90 days. Habits and expectations that form with the first hires are disproportionately sticky — the team culture at 10 people is largely set by what the first 3 people did. Changing from implicit-office to explicit-remote (or vice versa) after people have joined creates winners and losers among existing staff and is one of the most reliably friction-generating decisions a founder can delay.
