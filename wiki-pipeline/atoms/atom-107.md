## Claim

When nobody on your team can immediately name the person responsible for office logistics — supplies, lease, visitor access, safety — the process does not exist yet, and things will start falling through the cracks.

## Source quote or paraphrase

The practitioner at alexrodba.com describes the function as "operational glue" — the work that doesn't belong to any defined role but must get done. When it is nobody's job, it stays on the floor until it becomes a problem. In early-stage companies, "that's not my job" is not a statement that can be made — there are no clean handoffs, so if nobody picks it up, it doesn't get done.

## Why (inferred from source if not explicit)

At 2–10 people the office is small enough that individual frictions are visible but not yet urgent enough to force ownership. The failure mode is accumulation: nobody orders supplies → someone does it ad hoc → different people do it differently → costs are untracked → nobody knows what the office costs → nobody knows when the lease is up. Each individual failure is minor; the cumulative effect by the time you have 15–20 people is that facilities have no system and no owner, and inserting one retroactively is disruptive.
