## Summary

Practitioner contribution from an anonymous chief of staff (second or third CoS assignment; primary experience ~50–300 headcount). Structured extract aligned to the blog draft guest essay — planning, communication, decision rights, and process ownership. Strong themes: osmosis stops working past ~9–11 people; planning layers must match meeting cadence; information cascades up but often not down; informal CEO routing undermines line managers; leaders should play for the highest team; named process owners before changing frameworks; chief-of-staff triggers at growth complexity, not ~10 people. Opinion and pattern — not universal law. Practitioner review pending on blog draft.

Geographic context not specified; examples use generic headcount bands. Advice assumes venture-scale complexity (C-level, departments, external senior hires) — may need `conditional` flags at approval for bootstrapped or smaller orgs.

## Notable claims

- A process exists to get relevant information to decision-makers — look for ownership before new frameworks
- Split teams before Dunbar limits; the "one lunch table" myth outlasts the org chart
- Strategic / tactical / practical planning layers must match meeting cadence; weekly is not for strategic debate
- Running metrics (top line) vs change goals (department-owned problems); ~70% departmental capacity pre-booked
- Internal comms is a derivative of strategy; downward cascade on planning rhythm is often missing
- Target ~80% formal / ~20% informal information routes; ~40% informal in long-tenure orgs is a warning sign
- Leaders play for the highest team, not the lowest; return out-of-domain debates to domain owners
- One dashboard per department, max five KPIs; cross-team forums with need-to-know, red metrics, blockers
- Senior hire: three conversations (fit, potential without veto, culture without solo veto); three sparks decide
- CoS triggers: working foreman CEO, unprepared meetings, inbox crowding strategy; listen ~10 days first
- Every important process needs a named owner before change; successor owns rhythm when operator leaves
