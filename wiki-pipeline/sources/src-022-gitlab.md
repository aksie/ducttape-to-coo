## Summary
By 2016, GitLab's planning material had moved from a linked strategy document into a public strategy page. The page has a clear structure: Why, Mission, Goals, Sequence, Constraints, Assumptions, and functional 2016 goals. It combines narrative direction with concrete functional goals across Technology, Revenue, Marketing, Finance, Operations, Production Engineering, and Partnerships.

## Canonical source decision
This page is the canonical public strategy source for the era and is dense enough to stand alone. Linked Google Docs, product direction pages, and functional subpages are excluded to keep the extraction source-budgeted.

## Notable claims
- GitLab states a mission: change creative work from read-only to read-write so everyone can contribute.
- Company goals include broad adoption, product vision, progress for team members, independence, and preserving values.
- Strategy is sequenced: on-prem popularity, on-prem revenue, private SaaS, public SaaS, then all knowledge workers.
- Constraints are explicit, including founder control, independence, low burn, values, speed, and life balance.
- Functional 2016 goals translate company direction into Technology, Revenue, Marketing, Finance, Operations, Production Engineering, and Partnerships work.

## What changed since previous era
The 2015 handbook referenced strategy as a separate document and relied on daily workflow norms for alignment. By 2016, GitLab made strategy public and structured: mission, goals, sequence, constraints, assumptions, and functional goals all appear in one source. Planning moved from implicit/shared context to a written operating artifact.

## Distinctive quote
"Publish our processes and plans in the handbook."
