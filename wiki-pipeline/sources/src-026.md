# Company-Level Planning & Goals at Foundation — Practitioner Experience

At 1–5 people, you don't need anything complex like OKRs. You do need to be clear on your goals for the coming period, why you exist, why YOU are doing this, and what your strategy is. And you need a rhythm already in evaluating and adjusting them.

## What good looks like at this phase

- **Everyone in the company can name the top priorities for the quarter without checking a document**, so all work and energy goes toward only the most important things.
- **Everybody understands why these are the priorities.**
- **You don't drift into different priorities without realizing it. When you do change them, communicate with the team and explain why.**

## What you actually need to do

- **Agree with the other founders on why you're working on this problem as a company and how your approach differs from other companies'.**
- **Set 3 to 5 quarterly priorities with the whole team in one room.** Not 10, not "a few themes" — define three to five concrete things the company will achieve this quarter. Write them down. Everyone should be able to repeat them. Split them across 'Product and Tech', 'Go to Market', and 'Business Ops'.
- **Priorities are visible, not buried in a doc.** Use a whiteboard in the office, a pinned Slack message, or a single slide in the shared drive — the medium doesn't matter as long as it's impossible to miss. Put priorities where people already look: a pinned Slack message, office whiteboard, or Notion homepage. Pick one place and keep it current. If people have to hunt for priorities, they'll ignore them.

- **Run a 15-minute weekly priorities check with the full team.** What did we get done? What's blocking us? Does our work still serve the quarterly priorities? Adjust if needed. No slides, no prep.
- **With the founding team, discuss progress weekly or biweekly and whether these are still the right priorities. Only act on other things once you've agreed on a priority change.**
- **After each quarter, write a frank analysis of why things went differently than planned and what your priorities are for next quarter.** Send it to someone, even if it's just yourself. This prevents you from saying things like "this feature will launch in 8 weeks" for four months in a row. When priorities are only verbal, it's easy to lose track of what you committed to.
- **Kill zombie projects that don't serve the priorities.** At this stage, the biggest planning failure is not "bad strategy" — it's working on things that made sense two months ago but don't anymore.

## Warning signs you're behind

### Output quality
- **One founder subtly changes their view of the market and starts acting on it without explicitly agreeing on priority changes.** This is bad because others will still be working toward the old priorities while you're pulling in a different direction. It also sets a bad example: that you can change direction whenever you want without explicitly updating priorities.
- **People work on things that are good for the company but NOT the priority.** This is bad because you're too small to spread your impact across non-priorities.
- **You have a goal or priority, but no strategy.** (I will add a link to an explanation about strategy later.)
- **If you ask anyone on the team "what are we focused on right now?" and they hesitate, your planning isn't working yet.**
- **You find yourself telling potential investors and your network different priorities than the ones you've written down.** This is bad because it means you think priorities should have changed, but you haven't done the work to make that explicit — leading to people working on the wrong things.

### Founder time
- **The founders constantly have to explain to team members what to do.**

### Process entry
- **People are working on things that aren't in the priorities doc.** Not because they're being rogue, but because they didn't know the priorities existed or what they meant.
