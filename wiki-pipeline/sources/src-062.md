## Summary

Practitioner observation on the first in-house legal hire, typically a growth-stage (Series A/B) question. The failure mode is predictable: the new lawyer inherits an accumulated backlog, is plugged straight into the operational firehose, and burns out within a year while strategic risks go unaddressed. What good looks like is a deliberate diagnostic mandate for the first months, an experienced advisor on the side as sounding board and coach, and checklists so routine legal stays in the business.

The deeper point is cultural: lawyers are trained to flag everything, founders see legal as a blocker, and the first hire starts "3–0 down" — so the intangible coaching/boundary-setting side is exactly where outside help matters.

## Notable claims

- The first in-house lawyer commonly burns out within a year — inherits mess, drowns, never zooms out.
- Give the first months a diagnostic mandate (operational and strategic) before the operational firehose opens.
- An experienced advisor on the side is hugely valuable as a sounding board and to coach a junior lawyer on operating in-house.
- Much routine legal (e.g. standard NDAs) can be handled by the business with a good checklist.
- A lawyer alone often makes it worse by flagging everything; boundary-setting is hard, personal, and needs coaching.
- Founders perceive legal as a blocker, so the first hire must win trust while clearing the backlog.
