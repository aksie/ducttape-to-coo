## What breaks or goes wrong?

When customer-facing roles and engineering are both in the house but nobody has agreed **how** handoffs work in chat, you get a predictable fight — not because one side is wrong, but because each team is optimizing for something real.

**Engineering side:** "CS needs to stop mentioning us in random channels — we never get any work done anymore." Deep work dies when every customer question becomes an @-mention in `#general` or a DM.

**CS side:** "Engineers don't respond to mentions — customers are suffering." From CS's view, silence in `#bugs` or ignored @-tags means revenue and trust bleed out while engineering is "busy."

Both complaints can be true at the same time. The company cannot prescribe one universal channel map — product, on-call rotation, and team size all matter. What *is* missing is a **joint decision** both teams can work with: which channel (or ticket type) owns customer issues, when an @-mention is appropriate vs async queue, and what response window is realistic.

Leaving it as a morale or personality problem — "engineering is unresponsive" vs "CS is noisy" — lets the fight recur every quarter.

## What should you actually do?

When the discussion surfaces between teams, treat it as a norms decision:

1. Get CS and engineering leads in one room (30–45 minutes).
2. Name each complaint without debating who is worse.
3. Agree a shared path — channel list line, ticket-first rule, @-mention etiquette, response SLA — that **both** sides accept.
4. Write it in one paragraph, post it where both teams look, and revisit when headcount or product complexity shifts.

The right answer depends on context. The failure mode is not picking the "wrong" SLA — it's never making the decision explicit.

## Anything else?

Common trigger: first dedicated CS hire or first engineer who isn't a founder — handoffs that used to be verbal across a table now cross roles in Slack.
