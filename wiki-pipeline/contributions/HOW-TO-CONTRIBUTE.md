# How to contribute your experience — no GitHub required

This wiki is built on practitioner knowledge: things people learned by actually running operations at startups. If you've been a founder, COO, ops lead, or early employee at a growing company, you have things worth sharing.

You don't need to know anything about code, git, or markdown. Here are three ways to contribute:

---

## Option 1 — Fill in a form on GitHub (5 minutes)

This is the easiest option if you have a GitHub account (or are willing to create one — it's free).

1. Go to: [github.com/aksie/ducttape-to-coo/issues/new?template=practitioner-experience.md](https://github.com/aksie/ducttape-to-coo/issues/new?template=practitioner-experience.md)
2. You'll see a form with a few questions about a process you know well
3. Fill in what you can — free text is fine, you don't need to answer every question
4. Click "Submit new issue"

That's it. A maintainer will convert your contribution into the right format and add it to the wiki pipeline.

---

## Option 2 — Send a message or email

No GitHub account needed. Just write up your thoughts in an email, WhatsApp, or LinkedIn message and send it to the maintainer directly.

You can write in any format — bullet points, a few paragraphs, a brain dump. The more specific the better, but anything is useful.

Try to include:
- **Which process** you're writing about (e.g. hiring, office setup, financial admin, invoicing)
- **Company size** at the time (e.g. "we had about 15 people when we did this")
- **What worked** and **what broke** — both are valuable
- **Why** — what's the mechanism? What happens if you skip it?

---

## Option 3 — Fill in the template and send the file

If you're comfortable editing a text file, you can fill in the contribution template and send it as a file attachment.

1. Download `_template.md` from this folder
2. Open it in any text editor (Notes, Word, Google Docs — anything works)
3. Fill in the sections you have something to say about — skip the rest
4. Send the file to the maintainer

---

## What happens after you contribute?

Your contribution goes through the wiki pipeline:

1. A maintainer (or LLM using the extraction prompt) reads your text and pulls out individual claims
2. Each claim becomes an "atom" in the pipeline — a small, traceable unit of knowledge
3. Atoms from multiple practitioners and sources are synthesised into wiki entries
4. The maintainer reviews and approves claims before they go live
5. Your contribution is attributed in the wiki's sources section

Your name (or "anonymous") will appear as the source. You can request to be anonymous at any time.

---

## What makes a great contribution?

The most valuable things are the ones that are hardest to find anywhere else:

- **What breaks** — the failure modes, the red flags, the things that go wrong when you skip a step. Published "best practices" almost never include these.
- **The why** — not just "do X" but "we did X because Y was breaking" or "skip this and Z happens." The reasoning is what makes advice transferable.
- **Stage specificity** — what's true at 5 people is often wrong at 30. If you can say "this stopped working when we hit about 20 people," that's extremely useful.
- **First-person** — "we did this and it worked because..." is worth more than ten generic tips.

You don't need all of these. Even a rough contribution with one or two of them is valuable.
