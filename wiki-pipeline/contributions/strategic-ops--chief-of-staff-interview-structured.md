# Practitioner extract — Chief of Staff lens on scaling ops

**Scope:** One chief of staff's craft at roughly **fifty to three hundred people**, unless a section notes an earlier stage. Opinion and pattern — not framework canon.

**Cross-cutting thesis (knockout):** A process is an efficiency move to get as much relevant information as possible into the heads of the people who make choices. When alignment breaks, look for **who owns the process** before reaching for a new framework.

**Blog alignment:** Content below mirrors `blog/posts/drafts/what-a-chief-of-staff-sees-when-ops-stops-scaling.md` (guest sections only). Use this file for Phase 1b extraction → atoms → wiki addendums.

## Cross-cutting — Process ownership

*Maps to **1.7 Process Documentation**, **1.4 Who Decides What**, and every process where change fails.*

### First hires → early revenue (~2–15 → ~11–50)

**What good looks like**
- Founders still carry most process implicitly; informal ownership among a small team is fine.

**Signs things are broken**
- Process becomes "everyone's problem" — which means nobody's.
- Visionary founders treat process as an afterthought; nothing has a named owner.

### Growth → scaled (~50–300+)

**What good looks like**
- **Every important process has a named owner** — not "leadership," not "everyone."
- **Change the process only after ownership is clear** — and after the people inside it understand why.
- When a chief of staff or operator leaves, a **successor owns the rhythm** (e.g. medior chief of staff); execution does not revert to founder intuition alone within a quarter.

**Signs things are broken**
- Someone tries to improve planning, communication, or decision rhythm without a clear owner; daily reality shifts; people lose something; the initiative drifts back.
- Process changes create losers; people were comfortable with the old way for a reason.
- "We need OKRs" (or any framework) treated as root cause when the real gap is ownership.

**What you actually need to do**
- Before changing how planning, comms, or decisions work: name who owns the process, who maintains it after consultants or operators leave, and who carries information to decision-makers ongoing.

## Cross-cutting — Chief of Staff / operator entry

*Spans **1.2**, **1.3**, **1.4**, CEO calendar — not a single wiki process.*

### When to bring someone in (typically growth+, not at ~10 people)

**What good looks like**
- Trigger recognised: still the **working foreman/woman**; arriving at meetings **unprepared**; **behind on the facts** because strategy is losing to the inbox; small requests **filling the CEO calendar** while bigger decisions wait.
- First weeks: listen (~**ten days**), walk the org, then work on **formal routes**, **CEO preparation**, and **buffering** without leaving the CEO in the dark — roughly a week ahead on strategic conversations; Monday with delegation done and topics known.
- Founder keeps **energy-positive ownership** where it still gives them fuel — even when it no longer fits the formal role on paper; protect that deliberately, don't treat it as failure to delegate.

**Signs things are broken**
- Chief of staff role confused with PA; founder drowning in ops while still handling calendar minutiae themselves.
- Operator tries to nudge the founder off everything they care about — loses trust or the role.

## 1.1 Company-Level Planning & Goals

### First hires (~2–15)

**What good looks like**
- Strategy and pricing can still be a **conversation at the same table** (up to ~9–11 people) — alignment by osmosis.
- In first ~**15 hires**, prioritise **diversity of thinking** — hard to retrofit later. Survive product-market fit first.

**Signs things are broken**
- Founders assume everyone naturally works toward the whole; they see the whole, new hires see a slice.
- One function runs a heavyweight goal process copied from a book while another executive ignores the page.
- Key results invented from solutions, not problems.
- Tech and commercial living in **different planning languages**.

### Early revenue (~11–50)

**What good looks like**
- **Split teams deliberately** before Dunbar-ish limits (~9–11) rather than wait for politics to force it.
- Founders name that alignment will not happen "by itself" anymore.

**Signs things are broken**
- The **"one lunch table" myth** outlasts the org chart in the founder's head after it's physically impossible.
- Around **~50 people**, five teams run five different rhythms without anyone naming that the shared picture is gone.

### Growth (~50–150)

**What good looks like**
- **Strategic / tactical / practical** layers respected in meetings:
  - **Strategic** — ~12-month problems and chances → annual / rare deep forums.
  - **Tactical** — quarter → quarterly planning.
  - **Practical** — weeks → weekly forums.
- **Match meeting cadence to layer** — weekly is for practical work; avoid strategic debates there.
- Decisions largely made **before** the meeting; the meeting **confirms**, not discovers.
- **Running metrics** (company top line — MRR, NPS, eNPS, etc.) set from C-level with input on feasibility.
- **Change goals:** each department owns **3–5** real problems or chances at year horizon, then quarter.
- Departments **pre-book only ~70%** of capacity on their own objectives; rest is slack for cross-team dependencies.
- When two departments collide, **C-level resolves priority**.

**Signs things are broken**
- Strategic debate dropped into a weekly tactical meeting → unprepared room → rushed decisions → bad outcomes.
- Departments at **100% local load** → permanent gridlock on shared dependencies (marketing ↔ engineering ↔ legal).
- Friction visible by ~80 people: different functions at different planning maturity; C-level revisits operational choices that belong to domain owners.

### Scaled (~150–300+)

**What good looks like**
- Year strategy cross-aligned across departments after dependency negotiation.
- **Posters per department** — strategy visible quarters ahead; other teams can read each other's plans (practitioner preference; low-tech but works for them).

**Signs things are broken**
- Planning system abandoned after a failed push without ownership.
- "Half-smart" leaders add complexity to look important; smart leaders simplify.

## 1.1b Team Goals & Cascading Goals

### Growth → scaled

**What good looks like**
- Same **running metrics vs change goals** split as company planning: top line from C-level; departmental problems owned locally with **~70% capacity pre-booked**.
- Cross-team dependency slack built into departmental plans — not an afterthought.

**Signs things are broken**
- Information cascades **up** but teams execute on goals derived from a **stale or partial** picture.
- Tech runs heavyweight goal machinery while commercial runs on heroics — no shared planning language.

## 1.2 Internal Communication about Strategy

### First hires → early revenue

**What good looks like**
- Founders still tell the story often enough that the early team shares one mental model.

**Signs things are broken**
- **Multi-founder teams** explain direction **slightly differently** to their functions → unspoken frustration.
- Internal comms treated as separate from strategy — practitioner view: **internal comms is a derivative of strategy**; nobody owns downward transmission.

### Growth → scaled

**What good looks like**
- Cascade **down** on the same rhythm as planning — strategy, priorities, and context flow to teams after they flow up.
- Founder accepts telling the "same story" many times: in a **~50-person company**, each person may hear it **once a year** while the leader feels they repeat it weekly — that's not enough for the organisation; it's barely a start for the leader.

**Signs things are broken**
- Leadership articulates precisely **upward** in the management team; organisation below does not receive the downward cascade.
- Strategy lives in the founder's head and the investor deck; the org works from a plan **six months old**.
- If nobody owns downward transmission on the planning rhythm, the job isn't finished.

## 1.3 Operational Internal Communication Norms

### Growth → scaled

**What good looks like**
- Target mix: **~80% formal / ~20% informal** information routes.
- Informal allowed (walk-ins, skip-level) — but **bounded**.
- **Named owner** on streams that would otherwise land on the founder ad hoc — **legal and commercial "quick yes/no"** questions are the classic case — with a thin layer between small daily asks and CEO intuition.

**Signs things are broken**
- **~40% informal** routes in long-tenure organic companies — people go to the CEO when the line manager is slow or says no; CEO feels helpful and decisive; line manager authority undercut without anyone meaning to.
- Two people in the same C-level meeting with **100% vs 40% of the information** → decision pressed before everyone's ready → resentment.
- Commercial or legal bypass managers for speed ("CEO said yes in the corridor").

## 1.4 Who Decides What

### First hires

**What good looks like**
- Two founders can still align by talking; some "direction changes after the weekend" is acceptable at this stage.

### Early revenue → growth

**What good looks like**
- Leaders play for the **highest team**, not the lowest — e.g. a CRO aligns commercial team allegiance with C-level company allegiance; open disagreement with the CFO can be healthy; secretly carrying the functional team's vote into the room is not.
- When C-level debates something outside their domain, **return it to the domain owner** — e.g. "This doesn't touch revenue; it's the CPO's call."

**Signs things are broken**
- C-level executives defend their function as if representing a team vote; negotiations drip down the org.
- **People-pleasing managers** optimise locally (internal promotions and external hires — often from good intentions); the company pays in gridlock.

### Growth → scaled

**What good looks like**
- **Process steps around founder intuition** — a shower insight becomes a staged test, not a company-wide pivot by Monday.
- When conflict rises: **mediator-style** neutral questions to surface what's unsaid (practitioner trained in mediation).

**Signs things are broken**
- Trying to smooth **all** founder erratic energy away → lose founder trust or the role — the energy that built the company needs somewhere to go.
- Task-driven, low-empathy founders who believe **the best facts win** and won't move without full conviction — fine for product, brutal for a growing org. Mediation helps; it doesn't replace willingness to reflect.

## 1.5 Organizational Design

### Early revenue (~11+)

**What good looks like**
- Teams split before relationship overload (~9–11); conscious structure even if informal.

**Signs things are broken**
- **One lunch table myth** persists in the founder's head while the org has outgrown it.

## 1.6 Data & Reporting Infrastructure

### Growth → scaled

**What good looks like**
- Each department: **one dashboard**, at most **five KPIs**, reviewed **monthly** — quick read on organised vs chaotic functions.
- Debates **start from data**; if data isn't there, the meeting's job is to agree to get it — not decide on intuition alone.

**Signs things are broken**
- Some departments data-driven, others pure narrative — visible in dashboard discipline.
- Running-metrics obsession without departmental **change goals** — all top line, no owned problems.

---

## 1.8 Cross-Team Coordination & Collaboration

### Growth → scaled

**What good looks like**
- Cross-department forum (~dozen leaders), structured agenda:
  - **Need-to-know** for other departments
  - **Red metrics** (target vs actual and why)
  - **Blockers and interdependencies**
- **~70%** departmental plan capacity booked; **~30%** slack for dependencies.
- C-level sets cross-team priority when conflicts arise.

**Signs things are broken**
- Departments optimise locally; gridlock on shared dependencies.
- Meetings become status theatre without data or blocker resolution.

## 3.2 Recruitment & Hiring

### Growth → scaled (senior / C-level hires)

**What good looks like**
- **Three conversations** (practitioner's setup, not a universal standard):
  1. **Hiring manager** — role fit, hard criteria, chemistry ("spark joy" energy check).
  2. **One level up** — **potential only**: live case, whiteboard, unprepared problem. **No veto** — potential estimate, not final hire.
  3. **Long-tenure culture carrier** — coffee, life, work. Also **no solo veto**.
- The **three sparks together** decide — not one person's binary "not a fit."
- **9-box thinking** (performance × potential × values) as shared language, not bureaucracy.

**Signs things are broken**
- Culture interview returns binary "not a fit" with **veto power** — too subjective at scale.
- Specialist hire expected to be "friends" with every other head — wrong bar.

## Practitioner phrases (for synthesis)

- **Play for the highest team** — C-level allegiance above functional team.
- **80/20 formal/informal** — information routing target at scale.
- **Running metrics vs change goals** — top line from C-level; departmental problems owned locally.
- **70% departmental capacity booked** — rest for cross-team dependencies.
- **Strategic / tactical / practical** — meeting type must match horizon.
- **Three sparks** — hiring energy check across three conversations; no solo veto.
- **Ownership before change** — named owner before process change; successor before operator leaves.
