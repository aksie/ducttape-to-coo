# The first legal hire — what good looks like

*A practitioner contribution for the Duct Tape to COO wiki. A recurring theme from a Trailhead legal working session: how (and when) to bring legal in-house without burning out your first hire. Worth its own wiki entry.*

---

## What does good look like?

- **Use the first months to diagnose, not to firefight.** Spend the first period working out what is actually needed — operationally *and especially strategically* — before being plugged into the operational firehose. Paradoxically, the moment someone is hired full-time the floodgates open; a deliberate *"I'm only here for two months"* mandate forces a diagnostic posture instead of immediate drowning.

- **Keep an experienced person on the side, at a distance.** Someone who has done this before is hugely valuable — as a sounding board, someone to phone, and to teach a young lawyer how to operate inside a company. The intangible, "how do I operate in-house" side is exactly where outside help matters most.

- **Let the business handle much of the day-to-day legal itself.** A lot of "legal" work can be done by the business with a good checklist (e.g. standard NDAs don't need an expensive lawyer — unless it's an M&A NDA). This keeps the first legal hire free to zoom out.

## What should you actually do?

- Give the first legal hire an explicit **diagnostic mandate** for the first months (operational *and* strategic), rather than dropping them straight into the backlog.
- Equip the business with **checklists** so routine legal (standard NDAs, simple contracts) doesn't all route through one person.
- Line up an **experienced advisor on call** — a generalist who has done in-house before — as a sounding board and coach for a junior lawyer.

## What breaks or goes wrong?

- **The first in-house lawyer burns out within a year.** They inherit years of accumulated mess, drown in day-to-day work, and never get to zoom out — while the big strategic risks go unaddressed.
- **A lawyer alone often makes it worse.** Trained for 5–6 years to say yes to nobody, avoid risk, and solve everyone's problems, they flag *everything* — which doesn't scale. Coming in-house feels like it should be calmer, but clients are now literally at your desk; boundary-setting is hard, personal, and needs coaching.
- **You start 3–0 down with the founders.** Founders built the company, have strong views, and perceive legal as a *blocker* ("here comes the person who'll tell me it can't be done"). The first hire has to win trust back *and* clean up the backlog at the same time.

## How does this change as you grow?

This typically becomes a live question around Series A/B, when the volume and strategic weight of legal work outgrows founders + external counsel. Before that point, episodic external help (a trusted generalist) usually beats a full-time hire.

## Anything else?

- **Context:** NL/EU, non-regulated example company at roughly Series A/B. From a Trailhead legal working session.
- **Caveat:** This is the "intangible" side of the first legal hire — culture, boundaries, trust — which sits alongside the harder question of how legal risks map to the roadmap and the company's risk appetite. Not legal advice; practitioner synthesis for the wiki.
