## What does good look like?

When your legal filing is working well, you can respond to a data room request within a week — not because you scrambled, but because the files already exist in the right structure. Every key corporate document has a known home. The cap table is current, not reconstructed from memory. Contracts are filed with the signed PDF and a one-line summary of what it covers.

At the foundation stage, good looks like: deed of incorporation filed, shareholder register up to date, and a single folder where corporate documents live. That's it.

At first hires, good looks like: employment contracts in a known place (not in someone's email), a simple IP assignment clause in every contract, and freelance agreements with IP assignment for any contractor who touches code or content.

At early revenue, good looks like: annual accounts signed and filed (on time), tax filings complete and stored, a compliance calendar that tells you what's due and when, and a short list of your top contracts with a one-liner on each.

At growth, good looks like: a transaction-ready filing structure where legal, finance, and HR each have a clean directory. Anyone with access can find any document within two minutes without asking.

## What should you actually do?

**From day one (Foundation):**
- Set up a simple folder structure: `/legal/corporate/`, `/legal/contracts/`, `/legal/ip/`, `/legal/insurance/`
- File deed of incorporation and articles of association immediately
- Keep a shareholder register — even a one-tab spreadsheet is fine to start
- Register trademarks and domains early; note renewal dates somewhere
- File any co-founder agreements or vesting agreements as signed PDFs

**From first hire onwards:**
- Every employee gets a signed contract filed in `/hr/employment-contracts/[name]/`
- Every freelancer/contractor gets a signed agreement with an explicit IP assignment clause, filed in `/hr/freelance-contracts/`
- Keep a standard offer letter template and standard freelance template — update them when employment law changes
- Set up a `/compliance/gdpr/` folder: privacy policy (versioned), data processing register, DPAs with any processor that handles personal data

**From early revenue onwards:**
- Annual accounts: signed PDF filed within 2 weeks of signing off with the AGM (`/finance/annual-accounts/[year]-annual-accounts.pdf`)
- Tax filings: corporate tax, VAT, payroll tax — each in its own subfolder with the filed return and any correspondence
- WBSO applications and decisions filed in `/compliance/grants-and-subsidies/wbso/`
- Insurance: one PDF per policy, one-liner summary, renewal date noted in compliance calendar
- Maintain a `/legal/contracts/customers/` and `/legal/contracts/suppliers/` directory — not every contract, start with top 10 by value
- Keep a one-page authorized signatory document: who can sign what, up to what amount

**Compliance calendar (maintain as a simple spreadsheet or recurring calendar reminders):**
- Annual accounts filing deadline (within 5 months of financial year end for BV)
- Corporate tax filing (VPB)
- KvK annual filing
- Insurance renewal dates
- Trademark renewal dates
- Domain renewal dates
- WBSO application windows (twice yearly)
- Works council consultation obligations (if applicable)

## What breaks or goes wrong?

When preparing for a fundraising round, I applied the same instinct I'd developed from years on the buy side: a poorly organised data room creates a bad first impression, and a bad first impression makes investors more critical of everything that follows. So before opening the data room, I wanted the structure to look clean and considered for the stage we were at.

I thought I was well prepared. Then I went through everything with our external legal advisor — and discovered I wasn't. Missing addenda confirming salary changes. Contracts that hadn't actually been signed. Documents that existed but were filed inconsistently. It was a solid week of work to get things properly in order.

I was glad we caught it ourselves first. Had the investors' legal team found those gaps, it would have damaged our negotiating position — not because the underlying issues were serious, but because disorganisation signals risk. The likely outcome would have been either a lower price or more extensive reps and warranties. The cost of that week of preparation was a fraction of what it would have cost us at the table.

## How does this change as you grow?

At foundation, one person owns everything and the structure is minimal — the main thing is that it exists at all.

At first hires, HR documents start to accumulate. The risk is that contracts end up in a manager's email rather than a central location. Designating one person as "owner" of the filing structure prevents this.

At early revenue, formal financial documents (annual accounts, tax) start appearing. The bridge between formal financials and management financials becomes relevant — someone needs to document why the numbers in the statutory accounts differ from the management accounts.

At growth, the structure should be complete enough that a new CFO or legal counsel can get oriented within a day. The "08-transaction-prep" section activates only when a deal is live — it's not maintained year-round, just initiated quickly from a clean base.

## Tools and resources

- Google Drive or SharePoint for the filing structure (simple, accessible, no extra cost)
- A shared spreadsheet for the compliance calendar — low-tech is fine
- Notubiz or similar for board/AGM minutes management (Dutch market)
- Stripe Atlas guides cover the US equivalent of this structure well, useful for comparison
- For NL specifically: KvK, Belastingdienst portals — worth bookmarking and ensuring the right person has access credentials stored securely (not just in one person's head)

## Anything else?

This contribution covers the "always maintain" layer of transaction readiness — the corporate, legal, and compliance filing that should be current at all times, independent of whether a transaction is happening.

The "transaction-only" sections (financial deep-dives, tech DD, commercial/market analysis) are not covered here — those are prepared when a deal is active and are out of scope for day-to-day operations best practice.

Geographic note: primarily Dutch BV context (KvK, AVA, WBSO, BTW). The structure applies broadly but specific compliance deadlines and regulatory requirements differ by jurisdiction.
