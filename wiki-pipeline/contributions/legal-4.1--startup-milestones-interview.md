# Legal at the earliest stages — what to do, what not to do

*A practitioner contribution for the Duct Tape to COO wiki. Based on an interview about what "good" looks like for two founders building through first incorporation, first hire, and first outside funding — without turning legal into a full-time hobby before there is a business.*

---

## The balance you're aiming for

Most legal mistakes at the earliest stage are not "you forgot paragraph 14." They are:

- **Over-legalising before there is traction** — expensive documents, exotic structures, months at the notary while nobody wants the product.
- **Under-legalising once real money, people, or investors show up** — handshake deals, IP still with founders personally, no employment contracts, privacy held together with string.

The trick is to know **which phase you're in** and escalate paperwork in step with real risk — not with anxiety.

---

## Phase 0: Two founders building (pre-company)

### What good looks like

You have talked through the basics on a napkin (or over a beer): who does what, who puts in how much money, what you do if it clearly is not working, when you would pull the plug if you are down a certain amount. Nothing needs to be "super legal" yet — but **zero paper** among co-builders is also foolish.

You are building first. You are checking whether anyone wants what you are making. You are **not** playing company yet.

### What to do

- Sit down for an evening and agree a few scenarios: roles, money in, exit triggers if things go wrong.
- Agree explicitly: **once we see this is real, we will formalise** — entity, IP, founder terms.
- Keep it simple. No exotic holding structures because someone at a party suggested one.
- **Start a quarterly operations check-in from day one** — not when you have revenue, hires, or investors. Block an hour each quarter with a trusted ops/legal brain and walk a simple list against how the business actually works today. At pre-company the list is short (co-founder paper, what you're building, any early data or commitments). The same rhythm continues as you incorporate, hire, and sign deals — the checklist grows with the business, but the habit starts now.

### The quarterly operations check-in (from the start)

Once a quarter, block an hour and walk a simple list:

- Do we still have **light written clarity** on roles, money in, and exit triggers among co-builders?
- Are we still building toward something real — or **playing company** with lawyers and notaries too early?
- As the business grows: are **employment and contractor** papers fit for how we actually work?
- Do we have **written terms** with customers, suppliers, and key tech vendors — or only handshakes?
- Is our **privacy and data** setup still true for what the product does today?
- Is our **tech and data architecture** something we would be comfortable showing a professional investor — or "duct tape everywhere"?
- Are we creating a **tax or compliance** mess we will hate later?
- Is it time to **professionalise** any of the above, or are we still right to stay light?

This prevents the oscillation many product founders feel: panic ("we have nothing!") ↔ denial ("we do not need any of this"). You do not need to know everything you do not know — you need a **rhythm** from the start to surface it before due diligence or a big contract forces the issue.

### What not to do

- **Do not** spend thousands on a 80-page shareholder agreement before you know if anyone will pay a euro.
- **Do not** let legal structure eat the energy that should go to product and customers.
- **Do not** optimise for "what if my co-founder's partner does not like me" before you have validated the idea — that is a later problem.

> *"You start playing company while there is no company yet. Then you spend endless time on paperwork when you do not even know if you will earn one euro."*

## Phase 1: Traction — time to incorporate

### When to move

Move when there is real momentum: users, revenue, or serious interest from outsiders who want to invest or partner. That is when you choose a structure (often a Dutch BV, sometimes with a simple holding above it) and **before** the venture has a lot of value stuck in the wrong place.

If you wait too long, you may have to transfer valuable IP or goodwill into the entity later — messier and more expensive.

### What good looks like

- A clean entity exists.
- **All IP is in the company** (or the intended structure) — code, brand, product, content. Not "mostly," not "except the first commits."
- Founder terms are written down at a level appropriate to the stage — not necessarily bespoke from a top-tier firm on day one.

### What to do

- Incorporate when traction justifies it, not on day one of the idea.
- Do an **IP assignment / transfer** from founders into the company as part of setup. Whatever the product is — however small — belongs to the venture, not to an individual founder's laptop.
- Use a **sensible template** for founder/shareholder terms when friends, family, or first angels come in — principles and non-negotiables on paper beat "we have nothing, you tell us what to put in the contract."
- Keep the structure boring. Complexity is where expensive fixes live later. For a Dutch setting: templates from Capital Waters as the foundation are a great start. 

### What not to do

- **Do not** assume "IP never killed a deal" — keeping IP out of the entity is how you get unclean cap tables and DD pain.
- **Do not** pay premium legal fees for a full bespoke package at the first FFF round if a professional round is likely soon anyway — the next investor will often want their own documents. **Do** get the basics right: IP in, key principles documented.
- **Do not** adopt weird structures because one founder heard something at a networking event.

> *"Whatever happens — you want to be sure all IP sits in the company. Not a chunk still sitting with one of the founders."*

## Phase 2: First outside money (angels, FFF)

### What changes

The moment you give away a piece of what two founders built, you need more than a handshake. A third party with real money will expect professionalisation — and will **not** say "fine that you have nothing on paper."

This is a good moment for:

- Documented founder/shareholder arrangements (template-level is fine if traction is early).
- Clear cap table and shareholder register.
- IP already in the entity.

### What not to do

- **Do not** treat the first external investor like an internal co-founder conversation.
- **Do not** skip documentation because "they are friends" — that is when misalignment hurts most.

## Phase 3: First hire

### What good looks like

Everyone who joins gets **something in writing immediately** — at minimum a standard employment contract (or contractor agreement for freelancers). Not "we are all friends, it will never go wrong."

For digital/product companies: contracts include the basics you will need later (IP, confidentiality, and for employees the NL obligations that apply from day one).

### What to do

- Have a **standard employment contract template** ready before the first hire — use a startup-savvy lawyer or a reputable template, then stick to one stack of documents you reuse. 
- Same for freelancers who touch product: **written agreement + IP assignment**.
- For digital businesses collecting personal data: treat privacy/GDPR seriously **from minute one** — what you collect, why, how it is stored, processor agreements where needed. Copying terms from a comparable Dutch/EU competitor is a reasonable starting point if you adapt them honestly.
- Prefer one advisor or small firm that can cover employment, commercial, and privacy over a dozen one-off specialists in the earliest phase.

### What not to do

- **Do not** hire people with no contract because you trust the vibe.
- **Do not** collect user data for a year and only then ask "what should our privacy policy say?"
- **Do not** assume GDPR only matters at scale — for a data-heavy product, bad tech and bad privacy can end the company when a serious party looks under the hood.

> *"We are all good friends and it never goes wrong — until it does, and then you have a big problem."*

## Phase 4: First serious commercial relationships

There is no universal calendar ("every startup must have X by year two"). The quarterly ops check-in you started in Phase 0 is when customer, supplier, and vendor contracts become critical lines on the list — not when you first start the rhythm.

---

## When to bring legal help closer

You do not need a retainer on day one. You **do** need a path to someone who:

- Understands startups (not only corporate M&A).
- Will tell you when **not** to legalise yet.
- Can do a one-hour fix on an employment template as easily as a month-long engagement.

Good moments to tighten the relationship:

- First hire imminent.
- First external investment term sheet.
- First large customer contract where your side needs real terms.
- Product moves into regulated or data-sensitive territory.
- You are five years in and still have no management agreements — *that* is a cautionary tale, not a model.

Platforms that match companies with freelance legal specialists can work well for episodic needs; so can a single trusted generalist who covers employment, commercial, and privacy in the early years.

---

## The biggest failures (what to avoid at all costs)

1. **Legal instead of business** — you timmer everything shut, sit only with notaries and lawyers, and never learn if the idea works.

2. **Nothing on paper when it matters** — friends-and-family money with no documentation; hires with no contracts; suppliers and customers on vibes; management agreements missing years in.

3. **IP outside the company** — especially after incorporation and before professional investment.

4. **Privacy and tech debt for digital products** — data everywhere, no lawful basis, architecture a professional would refuse to inherit. Outcome: "rebuild it or we are not investing."

5. **Exotic structures early** — holdings, side arrangements, or founder deals that made sense at a borrel and nowhere else.

6. **Blind panic or blind denial** — no quarterly check on whether legal/ops still matches the business.

## How this maps to the framework

| Stage | Legal focus |
|--------|-------------|
| **Foundation (pre-traction)** | Co-founder understanding on paper (light); do not over-build; **quarterly ops check-in starts here**. |
| **Foundation (traction → BV)** | Incorporate; IP into entity; simple structure; template-grade founder docs if raising soon; same quarterly rhythm, growing checklist. |
| **First hires** | Employment/contractor templates; IP clauses; NL day-one obligations; privacy baseline for digital products. |
| **Early revenue / first angels** | Documented investor terms; contracts with key parties; quarterly check-in now includes commercial handshakes. |
| **Growth / professional round** | Transaction-ready filing, consistent contracts, privacy and tech that survive DD. |

## Caveats

- **Geography:** NL/EU bias — employment law, BV, GDPR. Adapt elsewhere.
- **Industry:** Digital/product startups discussed explicitly; a trades business has different privacy urgency.
- **Not legal advice:** Practitioner interview synthesis for the wiki; founders should get proper counsel for their situation.
