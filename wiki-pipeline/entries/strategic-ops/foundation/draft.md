# Company-Level Planning & Goals — Foundation (1–5 people)

> At the foundation stage, planning is not a system — it is a shared document and explicit daily prioritization norms. You do not need OKRs yet.

## What good looks like at this phase

<!-- claim-id: c-001 -->
<!-- atoms: atom-072-gitlab -->
- **Company-level planning can be a shared strategy reference plus clear daily prioritization norms.** When the team is small, alignment travels through a shared document and explicit work-selection heuristics. The overhead of a full planning system would likely exceed the coordination benefit.

## What you actually need to do

<!-- claim-id: c-002 -->
<!-- atoms: atom-073-gitlab -->
- **Write down how people should choose work: pick from the current milestone, keep issues actionable, and only assign work when you are actually starting it.** Early planning fails when the work list becomes aspirational clutter. A simple milestone and assignment norm keeps planning close to actual execution.

<!-- claim-id: c-003 -->
<!-- atoms: atom-074-gitlab -->
- **Create a lightweight commitment rule: do not make external promises without internal agreement, and keep the promises you do make.** At a small-team stage, over-promising is often a planning failure disguised as customer responsiveness. A simple promise rule protects focus and trust.

### Founder / key-person time

<!-- claim-id: c-004 -->
<!-- atoms: atom-072-gitlab (inferred) -->
- **The founder is fielding daily "what should I work on?" questions from the team instead of people using the strategy doc and prioritization norms to decide for themselves.** If planning context lives only in the founder's head, every work-selection decision requires a conversation.

## How this evolves next

> This section should be triangulated with another operator source. At present, only single-company handbook evidence is available.

<!-- placeholder: evolution atoms in this batch are for later phases -->
At First Hires (~5–25 people), strategy should become a structured artifact with mission, goals, sequence, constraints, assumptions, and functional goals in one place. The shared doc evolves into a public planning artifact that gives teams both direction and boundaries.
