# Company-Level Planning & Goals — First Hires (2–10 people)

> At First Hires, planning is not a system — it's a habit. The goal is shared understanding of what matters most right now, reviewed often enough that it actually drives decisions.

## What good looks like at this phase

<!-- claim-id: c-001 -->
Everyone on the team can tell you the company's top 3 priorities right now without looking anything up. Goals live in people's heads and in conversation, not in a document nobody reads.

<!-- claim-id: c-002 -->
You have one primary metric that tells you each week whether the business is working. You know it, your team knows it, and you use it to make decisions.

<!-- claim-id: c-003 -->
Goals are ambitious enough that hitting 70% is a good result — not so safe that you always reach 100%. If you're hitting everything, you're not setting real goals.

<!-- claim-id: c-004 -->
Goals are set at least partly by the team, not handed down entirely by founders. People know why the priorities are what they are.

## What you actually need to do

<!-- claim-id: c-005 -->
1. Write down 3 company goals for the quarter with a measurable result for each — not tasks, but outcomes you can score. Keep it to one page.

<!-- claim-id: c-006 -->
2. Share your thinking before asking the team to propose goals. Tell them what you know, what you're uncertain about, and what the company absolutely needs to get right. Then ask for their input.

<!-- claim-id: c-007 -->
3. Review progress on goals every week — not just every quarter. In your team meeting, ask: what progress did we make, what's blocking us, where do we need help?

<!-- claim-id: c-008 -->
4. Use your primary growth metric as the decision-making filter for the week. If something helps hit it, prioritise it. If it doesn't, it can wait.

<!-- claim-id: c-009 -->
5. At the start of each quarter, run a short retrospective: how did we do against last quarter's goals? Why? What do we do differently? Then set new ones.

## Warning signs you're behind

<!-- claim-id: c-010 -->
- Nobody on the team can name the top 3 company priorities without thinking hard or checking something.

<!-- claim-id: c-011 -->
- You don't know your growth rate right now. You have a general sense of how things are going, but not a number.

<!-- claim-id: c-012 -->
- You're consistently hitting 100% of your goals every quarter — they're probably too easy.

<!-- claim-id: c-013 -->
- You asked the team to propose goals without giving them any context first, and got back a list of 15 things that don't obviously connect to each other.

<!-- claim-id: c-014 -->
- Goals exist in a planning doc that was written at the start of the quarter and hasn't been looked at since.

<!-- claim-id: c-016 -->
- You are asking team members to set goals that ladder up to company strategy, but they were hired as individual contributors — the best designer or engineer — not for their ability to translate strategy into tactics. The goals you get back reflect their function, not the company's priorities.

## How this evolves next

<!-- claim-id: c-015 -->
At Early Scale (~25 people), informal goal-setting breaks down as multiple teams form with different focuses. You need a structured planning process with clear roles: who shares context, who proposes plans, who integrates and decides. The content of your goals doesn't change much — the process for setting them becomes more explicit.

## Tools & resources

<!-- claim-id: c-017 -->
- For a team of 2-10, consider OGSM (Objectives, Goals, Strategies, Measures) over OKR. OKR is process-heavy and light on strategy — OGSM forces you to connect the objective to a strategy before jumping to metrics, which is more useful at a stage where strategic clarity matters more than goal-tracking rigour.

- A shared Google Doc or Notion page with this quarter's 3-5 goals and their key results is enough at this stage
- Weekly team meeting with a standing agenda item: progress against goals, blockers, where we need help
- Quarterly all-hands devoted to reviewing results and setting new goals together
