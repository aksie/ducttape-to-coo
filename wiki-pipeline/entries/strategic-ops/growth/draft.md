# Company-Level Planning & Goals — Growth (~50–150 people)

> At growth stage, planning becomes an operating rhythm across multiple time horizons — not just a quarterly goal list.

## What good looks like at this phase

<!-- claim-id: c-001 -->
<!-- atoms: atom-080-gitlab -->
- **Planning operates as a cadence system across time horizons: week, month, quarter, year, strategy, vision, and mission.** Growth adds recurring planning events that cannot be coordinated from a single quarterly goal list. Cadence turns planning into an operating rhythm.

<!-- claim-id: c-004 -->
- **Planning respects strategic, tactical, and practical layers — and matches meeting cadence to each.** Strategic (~12-month problems) belongs in annual or rare deep forums; tactical (quarter) in quarterly planning; practical (weeks) in weekly forums. Weekly meetings are for practical work — not strategic debates nobody is prepared for.
  <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner), atom-336, atom-337 -->

<!-- claim-id: c-005 -->
- **Decisions are largely made before the meeting; the meeting confirms rather than discovers.** Confirmation meetings respect preparation time; dropping strategic debate into a weekly tactical slot forces rushed outcomes.
  <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner), atom-338 -->

<!-- claim-id: c-006 -->
- **Running metrics (company top line — MRR, NPS, eNPS, etc.) are set from C-level with feasibility input; change goals are owned locally.** Each department owns three to five real problems or chances at year horizon, then quarter — complementing top-line metrics with owned local improvement agendas.
  <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner), atom-339, atom-340 -->

<!-- claim-id: c-007 -->
- **Departments pre-book only ~70% of capacity on their own objectives** — reserving slack for cross-team dependencies (marketing ↔ engineering ↔ legal). When two departments collide, C-level resolves priority.
  <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner), atom-341, atom-342 -->

## What you actually need to do

<!-- claim-id: c-012 -->
1. **Before changing planning rhythm, name who owns the process and who maintains it after operators or consultants leave.** A process is an efficiency move to get relevant information to decision-makers — changing frameworks without ownership usually reverts within a quarter.
   <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner), atom-311, atom-317 -->

## Warning signs you're behind

### Output quality

<!-- claim-id: c-009 -->
- **Strategic debate lands in a weekly tactical meeting** — unprepared room, rushed decisions, bad outcomes. Wrong meeting layer wastes executive time and forces choices without context or data.
  <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner), atom-343 -->

<!-- claim-id: c-010 -->
- **Departments run at 100% local load on their own objectives** — permanent gridlock on shared dependencies. Cross-team work needs unbooked capacity; fully booked departmental plans cannot absorb interdependencies.
  <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner), atom-344 -->

### Founder / key-person time

<!-- claim-id: c-011 -->
- **By ~eighty people, functions run at different planning maturity and C-level revisits operational choices that belong to domain owners** — a signal that planning ownership was never pushed down.
  <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner), atom-345 -->

### Process entry

<!-- claim-id: c-002 -->
<!-- atoms: atom-080-gitlab (inferred) -->
- **The company only does planning once per quarter and everything else is ad hoc.** Without a cadence, important planning events (strategy review, vision check, annual planning) get squeezed out by the urgency of the quarter.

## How this evolves next

<!-- claim-id: c-003 -->
<!-- atoms: atom-081-gitlab -->
- **OKRs need to sit inside a broader company cadence with board meetings, sales targets, metrics reviews, yearly planning, strategy, vision, and mission.** OKRs coordinate goals, but they do not coordinate every recurring management process. A growing company needs both the goal system and the operating calendar to fit together.
