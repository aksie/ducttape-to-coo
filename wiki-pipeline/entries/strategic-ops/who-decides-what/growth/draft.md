# Who Decides What — Growth (~50–150 people)

> At growth stage, C-level leaders sit in two teams at once — their function and the company. The job is to align those allegiances, not to carry the functional team's vote into the room.

## What good looks like at this phase

<!-- claim-id: c-001 -->
- **Leaders play for the highest team, not the lowest** — e.g. a CRO aligns commercial-team allegiance with C-level company allegiance. Open disagreement with the CFO can be healthy; secretly carrying the functional team's vote into the C-suite is not.
  <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-002 -->
- **When C-level debates something outside their domain, it returns to the domain owner** — e.g. "This doesn't touch revenue; it's the CPO's call." C-level time should go to cross-cutting decisions, not re-litigating operational choices that belong to domain owners.
  <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner) -->

## Warning signs you're behind

### Output quality

<!-- claim-id: c-003 -->
- **C-level executives defend their function as if representing a team vote** — negotiations drip down the org instead of resolving at the right level. Functional advocacy in the C-suite prevents company-level tradeoffs.
  <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-004 -->
- **People-pleasing managers optimise locally** — common with internal promotions and external hires, often from good intentions — and the company pays in gridlock on cross-team priorities.
  <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner) -->
