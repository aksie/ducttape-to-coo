# Legal — First Hires (2 to 10 people)

> The moment you hire your first employee, Dutch employment law activates — fully, with no startup exemption and no headcount threshold. Most of the obligations are easy to meet if you know about them. Almost none of them are obvious.

## What good looks like at this stage

<!-- claim-id: c-001 -->
- **One person owns the filing structure, and all employment documents have a known home.** Contracts don't end up in a manager's email. Every new employee's signed contract, with IP assignment and confidentiality clauses, is filed the day they start. The founding documents from the previous stage are still current and findable.
  <!-- sources: src-038 (practitioner contribution) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-002 -->
- **NL compliance obligations are active from day one of employment.** Hour registration runs. A vertrouwenspersoon is designated. There are no retroactive gaps to fix.
  <!-- sources: src-034 (Netherlands Arbeidstijdenwet), src-035 (Arbowet) -->

## What you actually need to do

<!-- claim-id: c-003 -->
1. **Designate one person as the explicit owner of the filing structure before you make your first hire.** This person is responsible for ensuring every signed contract ends up in the right place. Without a named owner, documents default to email inboxes and are effectively lost. The owner doesn't need to be a lawyer — they just need to know what goes where and do it consistently.
   <!-- sources: src-038 (practitioner contribution) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-004 -->
2. **Start tracking working hours for every employee from their first day.** Dutch law (Arbeidstijdenwet) requires objective, reliable, and accessible time records for all employees, regardless of role or arrangement. Records must be kept for at least one year. This is not optional and not staged by headcount — it applies from the moment the first employment contract is signed.
   <!-- sources: src-034 (Arbeidstijdenwet / 2022 Dutch high court ruling) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-005 -->
3. **Designate a vertrouwenspersoon (confidential counsellor) before you reach 10 employees.** Dutch law requires this from 10 employees onwards, but putting it in place earlier prevents a compliance gap and sends a signal about culture. If you don't have someone appropriate internally, contract an external provider — this costs a few hundred euros per year and requires almost no ongoing management.
   <!-- sources: src-035 (Arbowet), src-038 (practitioner contribution) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-006 -->
4. **File every employment contract as a signed PDF the day the employee starts.** Include non-compete, non-solicitation, confidentiality, and IP assignment as standard clauses — not optional add-ons negotiated per hire. Keep a standard offer letter template reviewed by a lawyer once, and use it consistently. The same applies to freelance and contractor agreements.
   <!-- sources: src-038 (practitioner contribution) -->

## Warning signs you're behind

### Output quality

<!-- claim-id: c-007 -->
- **Missing hour registration exposes you to fines of up to €10,000 per employee.** The Dutch Labour Inspectorate enforces this actively. Many founders assume hour tracking is a growth-stage concern — it is not. The obligation begins with the first employment contract and the consequences of non-compliance apply immediately.
  <!-- sources: src-034 (Arbeidstijdenwet) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-008 -->
- **Dutch employment law compliance starts at the first hire, not at 10 or 25 people.** Hour registration, sick leave procedures, and non-discrimination policy are all legally required from the moment an employment contract exists. There is no startup exemption. Catching up retroactively — after an inspection, a dispute, or a due diligence — is possible but expensive.
  <!-- sources: src-033 (Arboportaal.nl / Dutch employment law summary) -->

## How this evolves next

<!-- claim-id: c-009 (evolution) -->
- **At Early Revenue, the filing structure you've built becomes the foundation for transaction readiness.** The compliance calendar (tax deadlines, insurance renewals, trademark renewals) matters more as obligations multiply. The question shifts from "do we have a structure?" to "is it complete enough that an external party could navigate it in a week?"
