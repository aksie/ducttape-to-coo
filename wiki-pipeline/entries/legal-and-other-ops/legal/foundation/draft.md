# Legal — Foundation (Founders plus maybe 1 person)

> At foundation, legal is about getting three things right that you can never redo cleanly: the founding documents, the IP ownership, and the filing structure. None of them take long. All of them compound badly if skipped.

## What good looks like at this stage

<!-- claim-id: c-001 -->
- **Founding documents filed and findable.** Deed of incorporation, articles of association, and a current shareholder register all live in a single known folder. The cap table — even a one-tab spreadsheet — is up to date. Any co-founder agreements (equity split, vesting, what happens if someone leaves) are signed and filed.
  <!-- sources: src-038 (practitioner contribution, no bias) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-007 -->
- **Co-founders have talked through the basics on paper — roles, capital, exit triggers — without heavyweight legal docs.** You are building and testing demand, not playing company with notaries and lawyers before a business exists. Zero paper among co-builders is as foolish as an 80-page shareholder agreement pre-PMF.
  <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-008 -->
- **A clean entity exists and all IP — code, brand, product, content — sits in the company, including work from the pre-BV period.** Execute an IP assignment from founders as part of incorporation; not "mostly," not "except the first commits."
  <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

## What you actually need to do

<!-- claim-id: c-002 -->
1. **Set up a minimal folder structure now, before anything gets lost.** Create: `/corporate/` (founding docs, cap table, shareholder register), `/contracts/` (any agreements you've signed), `/ip/` (trademarks, domains, IP assignments), `/insurance/` (when relevant). One Google Drive or Dropbox folder per section is enough. The point is that documents have a home — not that the home is elaborate.
   <!-- sources: src-038 (practitioner contribution) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-003 -->
2. **Register trademarks and domain names early, and record their renewal dates immediately.** Don't wait until you're bigger. Trademark and domain registrations lapse silently — there are no urgent reminders. Note renewal dates in the folder or a simple spreadsheet at the moment you register. A lapsed trademark or domain name is recoverable only slowly and sometimes not at all.
   <!-- sources: src-038 (practitioner contribution) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-004 -->
3. **Have every founder assign their IP to the company in writing before building anything.** This is a standard clause in a co-founder agreement or a separate IP assignment document. Without it, the IP legally belongs to the individual founder, not the company — which surfaces as a material finding in every future due diligence.
   <!-- sources: editorial (no current atom — gap for future Phase 1 run on co-founder agreements) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-009 -->
4. **Block an evening to agree co-founder scenarios and commit to a formalisation trigger.** Roles, money in, what happens if it fails — then explicitly: once we see traction, we incorporate, transfer IP, and document founder terms.
   <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-010 -->
5. **Incorporate when you have real momentum — users, revenue, or serious investor or partner interest — not on day one of the idea.** Escalate legal work in step with real risk (traction, people, money, data), not founder anxiety.
   <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-011 -->
6. **Keep your corporate structure simple — standard BV setup, no exotic holdings from networking advice.** Complexity is where expensive fixes live; advisors regularly see founders locked into arrangements they do not understand.
   <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-012 -->
7. **Once a quarter, block an hour to walk employment papers, customer/supplier contracts, privacy, tech/data architecture, and tax/compliance against how the business actually operates.** A fixed rhythm replaces oscillating between "we have nothing" panic and "we don't need this" denial.
   <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

## Warning signs you're behind

### Output quality

<!-- claim-id: c-005 -->
- **A disorganised data room causes disproportionate damage.** When investors or buyers review your company — even informally — the organisation of your documents signals how you run operations overall. A poorly structured data room makes them more critical of everything that follows, even if the underlying business is sound. This applies from your first angel round onwards.
  <!-- sources: src-038 (practitioner contribution, first-person M&A experience) -->

### Founder / key-person time

<!-- claim-id: c-013 -->
- **You commissioned extensive founder legal work or spend disproportionate time with notaries and lawyers before anyone will pay for the product.** Legal structure is eating energy that should go to product and customers — you are timmering everything shut before learning if the idea works.
  <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-014 -->
- **You swing between blind panic about legal gaps and blanket denial that documentation is needed — with no structured check-in.** Unstructured emotional response leads to wasted spend or due-diligence surprises.
  <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

### Process entry

<!-- claim-id: c-015 -->
- **Two co-founders are building together with no written understanding of roles, capital contribution, or exit triggers.** Verbal-only deals become expensive once money, IP, or investors appear.
  <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-016 -->
- **After incorporation, meaningful IP, code, or brand assets still sit with individual founders.** Do not assume "IP never killed a deal" — this drives unclean cap tables and due-diligence pain.
  <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-017 -->
- **Your structure includes exotic arrangements one founder adopted from informal advice without understanding them.** These become expensive to explain, fix, or defend in diligence.
  <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

## How this evolves next

<!-- claim-id: c-006 -->
- **At First Hires, compliance obligations begin — and you need a named owner for the filing before documents start defaulting to email.** Once you have employees, Dutch employment law activates (hour registration, sick leave procedures, vertrouwenspersoon). Designate one person responsible for the filing structure before the first hire, not after.
  <!-- sources: src-038 (practitioner contribution) -->

## Tools & resources

<!-- claim-id: c-018 -->
- **Use a reputable standard founder or shareholder agreement template as the foundation for early founder terms** — adapt to your principles rather than starting from zero or over-lawyering before friends-and-family money.
