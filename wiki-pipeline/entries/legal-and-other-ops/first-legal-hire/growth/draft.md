# The First Legal Hire — Growth (Series A–B)

> The first in-house lawyer usually burns out within a year — dropped into a backlog they never escape. Give them a diagnostic mandate first, keep an experienced advisor on the side, and leave routine legal in the business.

## What good looks like at this phase

<!-- claim-id: c-001 -->
- **The first months are a diagnostic mandate, not the operational firehose.** They work out what's actually needed — operationally and especially strategically — before being plugged in. A deliberate short mandate ("I'm only here for two months") forces that posture, because going full-time opens the floodgates.
  <!-- sources: src-062 (practitioner, Trailhead legal) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-002 -->
- **An experienced person sits on the side, at a distance.** Someone who has done this before is a sounding board to phone and a coach for a young lawyer on how to operate inside a company — the intangible "how do I operate in-house" skill is where they most need help.
  <!-- sources: src-062 (practitioner, Trailhead legal) -->

## What you actually need to do

<!-- claim-id: c-003 -->
1. **Keep routine legal in the business with good checklists.** Much day-to-day legal can be handled by the team — e.g. standard NDAs don't need an expensive lawyer (unless it's an M&A NDA). Routing every routine document through one person is what drowns the first hire and burns their strategic time.
   <!-- sources: src-062 (practitioner, Trailhead legal) -->

## Warning signs you're behind

### Founder / key-person time

<!-- claim-id: c-004 -->
- **The first in-house lawyer is on track to burn out within a year.** They inherit years of accumulated mess, drown in day-to-day work, and never get to zoom out — while the big strategic risks go unaddressed. That's both a people failure and a risk failure.
  <!-- sources: src-062 (practitioner, Trailhead legal) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-005 -->
- **The hire starts "3–0 down" and the founder relationship isn't managed.** Founders built the company, hold strong views, and perceive legal as a blocker — so the hire has to win trust back while clearing the backlog. If that relationship isn't actively managed, legal gets sidelined and the risks it was hired to catch get overruled.
  <!-- sources: src-062 (practitioner, Trailhead legal) -->

### Process entry

<!-- claim-id: c-006 -->
- **A lawyer left alone flags everything.** Trained for years to avoid risk and say yes to no one, they make things worse when isolated — and in-house, clients are at your desk, so boundary-setting is hard, personal, and needs coaching. Flagging everything erodes trust and turns legal into a bottleneck.
  <!-- sources: src-062 (practitioner, Trailhead legal) -->

## How this evolves next

<!-- claim-id: c-007 -->
- **Bringing legal fully in-house is typically a Series A/B question.** Before that point, episodic external help from a trusted generalist usually beats a full-time hire — hiring too early creates the burnout trap with too little strategic work to justify it. In-house makes sense once volume and strategic weight outgrow founders plus external counsel.
  <!-- sources: src-062 (practitioner, Trailhead legal) -->
