# Privacy & IP — Growth

> At growth, IP and privacy move from "know your exposure" to active protection: register the brand, decide where your software IP lives, close the back door, and train the people in front of it.

## What good looks like at this phase

## What you actually need to do

<!-- claim-id: c-001 -->
1. **Spend on protection where your edge actually is — for most companies that's trademarks, not patents.** Patents are usually low value unless your USP truly depends on something specific; trademarks matter because copycats appear, so register your mark and keep all registrations current. An unprotected, easily-copied brand is a red flag in due diligence.
   <!-- sources: src-061 (practitioner, Trailhead legal) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-002 -->
2. **Decide where your software IP lives and how it's licensed.** If you build in-house, protect it and register where possible; consider a separate software BV for a clean carve-out (it affects your legal structure and contracts). If you build for clients, license it — decide explicitly what IP you keep versus what transfers, and protect your IP in the client contracts.
   <!-- sources: src-061 (practitioner, Trailhead legal) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-003 -->
3. **Close the back door (CISO work) and train the people at the front.** Most big leaks are human error, so combine technical access controls with awareness training. Cyber insurance almost always requires awareness training anyway — together they give the lowest chance of real trouble.
   <!-- sources: src-061 (practitioner, Trailhead legal) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-004 -->
4. **Set rules for AI use.** At a bare minimum, don't upload customer or employee databases into AI tools — treat sharing with an external AI as a potential data leak, even on a paid enterprise account — and ideally have an AI governance policy stating how and when AI may be used.
   <!-- sources: src-061 (practitioner, Trailhead legal) -->

<!-- claim-id: c-005 -->
5. **If you develop or deploy high-risk AI, consult an expert.** The AI Act sets strict rules; for example, a recruitment tool that auto-screens CVs before a human is involved can be high-risk (discrimination risk). Building one yourself should trigger expert advice — a general scan can't resolve it.
   <!-- sources: src-061 (practitioner, Trailhead legal) -->

## Warning signs you're behind

### Output quality

<!-- claim-id: c-006 -->
- **Privacy is copy-pasted terms with no internal access controls.** Borrowed policies are not enough — without measures so not everyone can access all data, inadequate protection risks an Autoriteit Persoonsgegevens (AP) fine plus reputational damage, weighted by how sensitive your data is.
  <!-- sources: src-061 (practitioner, Trailhead legal) -->
