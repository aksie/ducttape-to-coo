# Accounts Payable & Expense Management — First Hires (2–10 people)

> At First Hires, expense management barely exists as a distinct process — but the habits you build now determine whether your books are clean or chaotic when it starts to matter. Get the basics right and you'll never have to fix them retroactively.

## What good looks like at this phase

<!-- claim-id: c-001 -->
Your expense categories are set up correctly from day one — matching your actual business model so your P&L tells you something useful. Retroactive recategorisation is brutal; every month you delay makes the eventual cleanup worse.

<!-- claim-id: c-002 -->
You have a clear payment approval threshold: everyone knows what they can spend without asking, and anything above that amount gets a second pair of eyes before it's paid.

<!-- claim-id: c-003 -->
You have an accountant engaged — not necessarily full-time, but someone you can call with questions like "Can our cash flow support a new hire?" or "How much should we set aside for taxes this quarter?" They should work with businesses similar to yours.

## What you actually need to do

<!-- claim-id: c-004 -->
1. Move all business expenses onto one or a small set of dedicated cards. Personal name or LLC name is fine — the rule is that no personal expenses mix in. At founder marginal tax rates (50%+), every missed deductible expense costs roughly half its value.

<!-- claim-id: c-005 -->
2. Track every expense from day one, even if it's just a log in a spreadsheet. Memory of why you spent $400 at a vendor decays fast — capture the context now or lose it at tax prep.

<!-- claim-id: c-006 -->
3. Get a bookkeeper earlier than you think you need one. They'll pay for themselves within 3 months in founder time saved — your job is to build the company, not categorise transactions.

<!-- claim-id: c-007 -->
4. Write down your expense policy and have every employee acknowledge it. Keep it to one page: what's covered, what needs approval, how to submit receipts. Verbal policies don't survive the first few hires.

<!-- claim-id: c-008 -->
5. Reconcile all bank accounts and credit cards every month. Match every card transaction to a receipt and a category before closing the month. With proper tooling this takes less than 2 hours; if you skip it, uncategorised transactions compound into a mess.

## Warning signs you're behind

<!-- claim-id: c-009 -->
- You can't quickly produce a receipt for any business expense the IRS or tax authority might ask about. If you can't find a specific receipt on demand, you'll lose the deduction plus pay penalties.

<!-- claim-id: c-010 -->
- You have an AP backlog you don't know about — invoices are piling up untracked, and you won't discover it until a vendor calls about a missed payment or a service gets cut off.

<!-- claim-id: c-011 -->
- Multiple people are charging expenses on a shared company card with no per-person visibility. You find out what was spent at the end of the month, after the money is already gone.

## How this evolves next

<!-- claim-id: c-012 -->
At Early Scale (~15-25 people), expense management becomes a real process: you'll need defined approval workflows (who approves what amount), corporate cards for regular spenders, and every invoice coded to the right cost centre before it's paid. The informal habits you built at First Hires become the foundation for these controls.

## Tools & resources

- A dedicated business credit card (or small set of cards) — no personal expense mixing
- A simple expense log or spreadsheet if you're pre-tooling; Pleo, Spendesk, or Ramp once the team grows
- A bookkeeper (part-time or outsourced) engaged before you think you need one
- A one-page written expense policy: thresholds, categories, receipt requirements
