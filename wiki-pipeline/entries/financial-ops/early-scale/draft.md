# Financial Operations — Early Scale (11–25 people)

> At early scale, financial operations shifts from survival-mode cash tracking to a repeatable system that gives you and your team real visibility. The goal is not sophisticated finance — it's reliable, timely numbers that inform decisions.

## What good looks like at this phase

<!-- claim-id: c-001 -->
You have a live financial model that you update and review regularly — not a document that exists only for fundraising decks.

<!-- claim-id: c-002 -->
Your monthly close is completed within 5 business days of month end, every month. The books are never more than 4 weeks behind.

<!-- claim-id: c-003 -->
Cash position and months of runway are visible to the full leadership team, not just the CEO. Everyone making spending decisions understands the constraints.

<!-- claim-id: c-004 -->
Gross margin is calculated and reported from your first month of revenue. You know whether it is trending in the right direction.

<!-- claim-id: c-005 -->
COGS and operating expenses are separated in your chart of accounts, giving you a clean gross margin line without analytical overhead.

<!-- claim-id: c-006 -->
Your chart of accounts reflects your actual business model and has been stable for at least 6 months. Historical comparisons are meaningful.

## What you actually need to do

<!-- claim-id: c-007 -->
1. Run a rolling 13-week cash flow forecast, updated weekly. Include current cash, projected cash in 4 and 13 weeks, and the key assumptions driving the numbers. Keep it simple enough that it actually gets updated.

<!-- claim-id: c-008 -->
2. Do a monthly variance review — actual vs. forecast — and write a short explanation for any line that missed by more than 10%. The explanation matters more than the number.

<!-- claim-id: c-009 -->
3. Run a monthly P&L review with the founding team. Put it in the calendar. Make it a standing meeting, not an ad hoc one.

<!-- claim-id: c-010 -->
4. Get a bookkeeper if you don't have one. The founder time saved in the first 3 months typically exceeds the cost. Feed them clean data: receipts in, invoices out, payroll records on time.

<!-- claim-id: c-011 -->
5. Document your expense policy and have every employee sign it. A verbal policy doesn't survive growth past 15 people and fails diligence every time.

<!-- claim-id: c-012 -->
6. Complete a bank reconciliation every month. Errors found monthly are small and quick to fix; errors found quarterly have compounded into something harder to untangle.

## Warning signs you're behind

<!-- claim-id: c-013 -->
- Your books are more than 4 weeks behind and you're making spending or hiring decisions based on bank balance.

<!-- claim-id: c-014 -->
- You've never done a budget vs. actuals review, or the first time you tried, you couldn't make sense of the categories because they've been applied inconsistently.

<!-- claim-id: c-015 -->
- You treat the bank balance as a proxy for financial health. If you've ever been surprised that cash was fine but the business was actually losing money (or vice versa), this is the symptom.

<!-- claim-id: c-016 -->
- Your expense categories are inconsistent across months, making historical data unreliable for forecasting.

## How this evolves next

<!-- claim-id: c-017 -->
At Growth stage, financial operations shifts from founder-managed or outsourced to a dedicated internal finance function — typically triggered by Series A close or reaching 30-40 people, whichever comes first.

<!-- claim-id: c-018 -->
Department heads begin owning their own budget lines. The finance function shifts from doing the work to enabling accountability and providing visibility.

## Tools & resources

- **Cash forecast:** A simple shared spreadsheet reviewed weekly is sufficient — complexity is the enemy of consistency at this stage.
- **Bookkeeping:** Bench, Pilot, or a local bookkeeper. The key criterion is API/automation compatibility so it scales later.
- **Investor reporting (if applicable):** Visible.vc, Briefcase, or a consistent Google Slides template. Consistency matters more than the tool.
- **Accounting software:** QuickBooks, Xero, or equivalent. Set up correctly from the start; migrations are painful.
