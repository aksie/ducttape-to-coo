# Expense Management — Foundation (1–5 people)

> At the foundation stage, your expense policy should be a principle, not a rulebook. Trust and speed matter more than formal control.

## What good looks like at this phase

<!-- claim-id: c-001 -->
<!-- atoms: atom-067-gitlab, atom-071-gitlab -->
- **Your expense policy can be a simple principle: spend company money as carefully as your own, without requiring pre-approval for every purchase.** When the team is small, trust and speed matter more than formal control. A principle lets people buy what they need without turning founders or managers into approval bottlenecks. The policy works because the organization is small enough for context, mistakes, and edge cases to travel quickly through conversation.

## What you actually need to do

<!-- claim-id: c-002 -->
<!-- atoms: atom-068-gitlab -->
- **Give people a lightweight escalation norm: if they are unsure whether an expense is appropriate, inform their manager before or soon after the purchase.** This preserves speed for obvious purchases while creating a low-friction path for judgment calls. It is not a formal approval workflow yet; it is a backstop for ambiguity.

<!-- claim-id: c-003 -->
<!-- atoms: atom-069-gitlab -->
- **Set a simple expense reporting deadline and a hard rule for personal spend on company cards.** Even with a trust-based policy, receipts need to arrive on a predictable cadence, and accidental personal use cannot linger unresolved. For example: expense reports within seven days after quarter-end, and personal card charges reported quickly and refunded within 14 days.

## Warning signs you're behind

> Warning signs for this cell should be triangulated with another operator source before publication. At present, only single-company handbook evidence is available.

### Process entry

<!-- claim-id: c-004 -->
<!-- atoms: atom-067-gitlab (inferred) -->
- **People are asking for permission before buying something obviously needed for work.** If your principle-based policy is not clear, people revert to asking first — which defeats the purpose of trusting them.

### Output quality

<!-- claim-id: c-005 -->
<!-- atoms: atom-069-gitlab (inferred) -->
- **Expense reports are consistently late or missing, or personal charges on company cards sit unresolved for weeks.** The lightweight policy only works when the basic hygiene still happens.

## How this evolves next

<!-- placeholder: no evolution atoms in this batch -->
At First Hires (~5–25 people), a principle-based policy usually needs to become a written policy with clearer categories: what is reimbursable, what needs pre-approval, and what expense reporting tool to use. The escalation norm becomes a formal approval workflow for larger purchases.

## Tools & resources

<!-- claim-id: c-006 -->
<!-- atoms: atom-070-gitlab -->
- **For a remote-first early team, focus reimbursement on enabling work: laptop, peripherals, headset, desk, chair, internet, phone, and office space if home is impractical.** At this phase, the useful policy question is not procurement optimization; it is whether people have the basic setup needed to work effectively.
