# Onboarding — First Hires

> At First Hires, onboarding is the first test of whether the company is what you said it was during recruiting. A bad first week is very hard to recover from — but a great one sets the tone for everything that follows.

## What good looks like at this stage

<!-- sources: src-014 (firstround_review) -->
Everything the new hire needs is ready before they walk in on day one — laptop set up, accounts provisioned, desk prepared. If they have to chase anyone to get started that would be a bad use of their time and a bad example of how the team works.

<!-- sources: src-014 (firstround_review) -->
The new hire's first day is structured and over-scheduled — they have a calendar full of conversations and context, not blank time with no one to talk to. Do make sure you schedule breaks, a first day gives a lot of input, don't overload it so it becomes too much to deal with.

<!-- sources: src-015 (firstround_review) -->
A founder or senior leader personally delivers the company's origin story, values, and how you work — not a junior team member or an HR deck. When founders articulate values directly, they carry far more weight than something written on paper.

<!-- sources: src-014 (firstround_review) -->
There is a written document — even a simple one — that captures what the company is, where it came from, and how people work here. New hires can read it before they start and return to it when they're confused. Written culture scales better than verbal transmission.

## What you actually need to do

1. Create a checklist of everything that needs to happen before day one: laptop ordered, accounts set up, contracts signed, desk ready. Run it every time without exception.
   <!-- sources: src-014 (firstround_review), src-016 (gitlab_handbook, gitlab_scale_bias) -->

2. Block out the new hire's first week in advance — schedule conversations with everyone they'll work with and at least one meeting with you on day one. Don't leave them to figure out who to talk to.
   <!-- sources: src-014 (firstround_review) -->

3. Design one thing into the first few days that makes the new hire easy to approach — a team lunch, an introduction at the team standup, asking them to share a fun fact. Don't leave the burden of making connections entirely on them, because people don't do this for themselves as proactively as you'd think.
   <!-- sources: src-015 (firstround_review) -->

4. Introduce them to every function in the company, even briefly — 30 minutes with someone from each team. At 2-10 people this is easy and still often skipped; doing it early prevents factions forming later.
   <!-- sources: src-014 (firstround_review, series_a_plus_bias) | flags: conditional -->

5. Run a 30-day check-in: what's going well, what's confusing, what do you wish you'd known earlier. Use the answers to improve onboarding for the next hire.
   <!-- sources: src-016 (gitlab_handbook, gitlab_scale_bias) -->

## Warning signs you're behind

<!-- sources: src-014 (firstround_review) -->
- The new hire's laptop wasn't ready, their accounts took days to set up, or they had blank time on day one with nothing to do and no one to talk to. A bad first impression is formed before any conversation happens and is very hard to walk back.

<!-- sources: src-016 (gitlab_handbook, gitlab_scale_bias) -->
- Your onboarding "process" lives entirely in the head of whoever last ran it — if that person is busy or on leave, the next new hire gets a significantly worse experience.

<!-- sources: src-014 (firstround_review) -->
- The experience you delivered in the first week doesn't match the experience you sold during recruiting. The new hire is already recalibrating their expectations downward.

## How this evolves next

<!-- sources: src-014 (firstround_review, series_a_plus_bias) -->
At Early Scale (~15-25 people), you're hiring often enough that onboarding needs to run without the founder in the room. The checklist becomes a process, a potential culture document becomes a living resource. Otherwise this will be too time consuming for a founder compared to their other concerns.

## Tools & resources

<!-- sources: src-014 (firstround_review), src-016 (gitlab_handbook, gitlab_scale_bias) -->
- A simple onboarding checklist in Notion or Google Docs — one page, runs before every hire
- A culture document: origin story, values, how we work, common questions. Start with one page and let it grow
- Calendar blocking: over-schedule week one, not under-schedule it
