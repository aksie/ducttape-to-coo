# Onboarding — First Hires

> At First Hires, onboarding is the first test of whether the company is what you said it was during recruiting. A bad first week is very hard to recover from — but a great one sets the tone for everything that follows.

## What good looks like at this stage

- **Everything is ready before the new hire walks in.**
  Laptop set up, accounts provisioned, desk prepared. If they have to chase anyone to get started, that's a bad use of their time and a bad example of how the team works.
  <!-- sources: src-014 (firstround_review) -->

- **First day is structured and over-scheduled.**
  A calendar full of conversations and context, not blank time with no one to talk to. Do schedule breaks — a first day gives a lot of input and you don't want to overload it.
  <!-- sources: src-014 (firstround_review) -->

- **A founder personally delivers the origin story and values.**
  Not a junior team member or an HR deck. When founders articulate values directly, they carry far more weight than something written on paper.
  <!-- sources: src-015 (firstround_review) -->

- **There is a written culture document, even a simple one.**
  What the company is, where it came from, how people work here. New hires can read it before they start and return to it when confused. Written culture scales better than verbal transmission.
  <!-- sources: src-014 (firstround_review) -->

## What you actually need to do

- **Create a pre-day-one checklist and run it every time.**
  Laptop ordered, accounts set up, contracts signed, desk ready. No exceptions.
  <!-- sources: src-014 (firstround_review), src-016 (gitlab_handbook, gitlab_scale_bias) -->

- **Block out the new hire's first week in advance.**
  Schedule conversations with everyone they'll work with and at least one meeting with you on day one. Don't leave them to figure out who to talk to.
  <!-- sources: src-014 (firstround_review) -->

- **Design something that makes the new hire easy to approach.**
  A team lunch, an introduction at standup, asking them to share a fun fact. Don't leave the burden of making connections entirely on them — people don't do this for themselves as proactively as you'd think.
  <!-- sources: src-015 (firstround_review) -->

- **Introduce them to every function in the company.**
  Even briefly — 30 minutes with someone from each team. At 2-10 people this is easy and still often skipped. Doing it early prevents factions forming later.
  <!-- sources: src-014 (firstround_review, series_a_plus_bias) | flags: conditional -->

- **Run a 30-day check-in.**
  What's going well, what's confusing, what do you wish you'd known earlier. Use the answers to improve onboarding for the next hire.
  <!-- sources: src-016 (gitlab_handbook, gitlab_scale_bias) -->

## Warning signs you're behind

### Output quality

- **The first week doesn't match the recruiting experience.**
  The new hire is already recalibrating their expectations downward. Recruiting creates a promise; onboarding is the first test of whether it was real.
  <!-- sources: src-014 (firstround_review) -->

### Founder / key-person time

- **Onboarding lives in one person's head.**
  If that person is busy or on leave, the next new hire gets a significantly worse experience. A documented checklist prevents this single point of failure.
  <!-- sources: src-016 (gitlab_handbook, gitlab_scale_bias) -->

### Process entry

- **Laptop wasn't ready, accounts took days, blank time on day one.**
  A bad first impression is formed before any conversation happens and is very hard to walk back. The new hire has nothing to do and no one to talk to.
  <!-- sources: src-014 (firstround_review) -->

## How this evolves next

- **At Early Scale (~15-25 people), onboarding runs without the founder.**
  The checklist becomes a process, a culture document becomes a living resource. Otherwise this will be too time consuming for a founder compared to their other concerns.
  <!-- sources: src-014 (firstround_review, series_a_plus_bias) -->

---

## Tools & resources

- A simple onboarding checklist in Notion or Google Docs — one page, runs before every hire
- A culture document: origin story, values, how we work, common questions. Start with one page and let it grow
- Calendar blocking: over-schedule week one, not under-schedule it
