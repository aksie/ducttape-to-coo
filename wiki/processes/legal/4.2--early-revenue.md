# Office & Facilities — Early Revenue (11–25 people)

> At Early Revenue, the ad-hoc approach that worked at 2–10 people starts to break. You need a named facilities owner, a real work location policy, and — if you have NL employees — your compliance foundations are now in active use, not just written down.

## What good looks like at this stage

- **You have a committed workspace with a named person responsible for it.**
  At 11–25 people, the optionality play of full flexibility is behind you. What good looks like is not a specific arrangement — dedicated office, co-working with assigned desks, or fully remote with documented expectations — but that there is a deliberate one: a consistent place, a named owner, and no ongoing uncertainty about where the team works.
  <!-- sources: src-027 (HN: remote vs in-person), src-028 (HN: colocation data), src-029 (alexrodba.com) -->

- **Your handbook is alive and people actually consult it.**
  The document that was a few pages for the first hire is now the reference for 20 people who can't all ask the founder. What good looks like: someone updates it when a policy changes, new hires read it before day one, and "what is our sick leave process?" gets answered with "check the handbook."
  <!-- sources: src-037 (Basecamp handbook) -->

- **Publishing your handbook publicly is a stronger recruiting signal now than at First Hires.**
  You're hiring faster and competing harder for talent. Candidates who read your handbook and still apply self-select more reliably than those who discover the culture post-offer. At this stage you still can't compete on brand or salary — but transparency about how you work is free and effective differentiation.
  <!-- sources: src-037 (Basecamp handbook) -->

## What you actually need to do

1. **Formalize your work location policy — a sentence in the offer letter is no longer enough.**
   At 11–25 people, your policy determines who you can hire, what the office is actually for, and what your employer obligations are across different geographies. Write it as a proper document: where work happens by default, expectations for in-office days if hybrid, rules for cross-border remote (timezones, countries, EOR requirements), and who owns the policy. Changing it after 15 people have joined on different expectations is expensive in trust and sometimes in contracts.
   <!-- sources: src-027 (HN: remote vs in-person), src-028 (HN: colocation data) -->

2. **Assign a named facilities owner.**
   At 11–25 people, office logistics — supplies, lease or membership, maintenance contacts, visitor access — are too much for "someone will handle it." One person, explicitly: it can be an operations generalist for whom facilities is one of several responsibilities. The task just needs an owner that everyone knows about.
   <!-- sources: src-029 (alexrodba.com) -->

3. **[NL] Your hour registration system should be in active use — audit it now if it isn't.**
   The Arbeidstijdenwet obligation that started with your first hire doesn't get easier to enforce retroactively as you grow. At 11–25 people, confirm that every employee is recording hours consistently, someone is approving timesheets, and records are being retained for at least one year. Records must be objective, reliable, and accessible.
   <!-- sources: src-034 (business.gov.nl — official) -->

4. **[NL] Revisit your sick leave procedure — by now you've probably had at least one sick employee.**
   Review the written WVP procedure against what actually happened. Is there insurance in place for long-term sick leave? The two-year pay obligation is a growing financial risk as headcount grows — one long-term sick employee at 15 people is 7% of payroll with no revenue contribution.
   <!-- sources: src-033 (research note), src-036 (octagonpeople.com, advisory — flagged) -->

5. **[NL] You must have a "vertrouwenspersoon" — this is no longer a plan, it is a current legal obligation.**
   At 10 or more employees, Dutch law requires a designated confidential advisor. At Early Revenue (11–25 people) you are over that threshold. If you don't have one yet, consider contracting an external provider.
   <!-- sources: src-035 (arboportaal.nl — official) -->

6. **[NL] Consider conducting a quick RI&E (Risico-inventarisatie en -evaluatie) for your workspace, if you have one.**
   The Arbowet requires this from the first hire, but with a real office and 11–25 people it becomes more important. At ≤25 employees you can do a simplified version yourself using a recognised instrument — no external safety consultant required. Scope for a tech office: ergonomics, emergency exit, first aid kit, fire safety.
   <!-- sources: src-030 (KVK — official) -->

## Warning signs you're behind

### Output quality

- **[NL] Hour registration is inconsistent or absent across your team.**
  At 11–25 employees, non-compliance with the Arbeidstijdenwet exposes you to fines of up to €10,000 per employee. The Labour Inspectorate audits without advance notice. If records aren't complete and consistent for every employee, reconstruct and fix them now.
  <!-- sources: src-034 (business.gov.nl — official) -->

- **[NL] A sick employee's WVP milestones are not being tracked.**
  At Early Revenue you have likely had or will soon have a long-term sick employee. Missing key milestones — problem analysis by week 6, plan of approach by week 8, UWV notification by week 42 — can trigger a loonsanctie: a mandatory one-year extension of the two-year pay obligation. Assign someone to own the WVP calendar the moment an employee is off sick for more than a few days.
  <!-- sources: src-033 (research note) -->

### Process entry

- **Nobody on your team can name the person responsible for office logistics.**
  At 11–25 people, this is a coordination failure, not charming startup informality. Supplies run out, maintenance requests go unfiled, visitors get lost, because nobody owns the function. Assign a facilities owner before the next person joins.
  <!-- sources: src-029 (alexrodba.com) -->

## How this evolves next

- **From ~25–50 people (Growth stage) at one location, facilities becomes a real function.**
  At Early Revenue it is one person's side responsibility and a handful of policies. At Growth it acquires scope: a formal lease or multiple locations, maintenance contracts, health & safety compliance at scale, visitor and contractor access procedures, and likely a dedicated facilities or operations manager. Build that then, not now — the Early Revenue version is ownership, the legal minimum, and documented decisions.
  <!-- sources: src-029 (alexrodba.com), src-030 (KVK) -->

## Tools & resources

- **Public handbook examples worth studying:** Basecamp (14 chapters, plain English, [GitHub repo](https://github.com/basecamp/handbook)) is the best model for teams up to ~30 people. GitLab's handbook (2,200+ pages) is the reference for thoroughness at scale. Both are public. Assign a named owner who reviews and updates the handbook at least quarterly — at 11–25 people, an out-of-date handbook erodes trust faster than no handbook at all.
