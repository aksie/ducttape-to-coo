# Office & Facilities — First Hires (2–10 people)

> At First Hires, this process is mostly documentation: a written work-location policy, an employee handbook, and a handful of Dutch legal obligations that apply from day one. Physical office management barely exists yet — but the compliance foundations do.

## What good looks like at this stage

- **Your default workplace setup is flexible, not fixed.**
  Coworking or flex space preserves capital and optionality at the stage when your headcount trajectory is most uncertain. A dedicated office is a commitment that makes sense later; at 2–10 people, the ability to change your mind is worth more than the permanence.
  <!-- sources: src-028 (HN: colocation data), src-031 (gable.to, vendor — corroborated) -->

- **You have a short, readable employee handbook that people actually consult.**
  Not a comprehensive legal document — a few pages that cover where people work, how they report sick, how they submit expenses, and who to call when something breaks. A handbook that fits in one read is more valuable than one that covers every edge case but sits unread.
  <!-- sources: src-037 (Basecamp handbook) -->

- **Publishing your handbook publicly is a recruiting signal.**
  Basecamp, GitLab, Remote.com, and Zapier all do it. Candidates who read it and still want to join self-select more reliably than those who discover the culture post-hire. At the first-hires stage, you can't compete on brand — transparency about how you work is free differentiation.
  <!-- sources: src-037 (Basecamp handbook) -->

## What you actually need to do

1. **Write down your work location policy before the first hire joins.**
   Fully remote, office-first, or hybrid with specific expectations — one or two sentences is enough. Share it in the offer letter or onboarding doc. The policy shapes who you can hire and what cultural habits form in the first 90 days. Changing it retroactively after people have joined on different expectations is expensive in trust and sometimes in attrition. If going remote: also specify which timezones and countries work — some are expensive or legally complex to hire into, and you'll want to be able to physically meet people without it becoming a 24-hour travel commitment.
   <!-- sources: src-027 (HN: remote vs in-person), src-028 (HN: colocation data) -->
   <!-- sources: human:aksie | flags: unverified -->

2. **[NL] Set up hour registration from your first NL employee — this is a legal obligation, not optional.**
   Dutch law (Arbeidstijdenwet) requires objective, reliable, and accessible time records for all employees including remote and flexible workers. Name the tool, who approves timesheets, and the submission deadline (e.g. weekly by Friday) in your handbook. Records must be kept for at least one year and employees must have access to their own. Keep it pragmatic: a simple tool like TimeChimp works fine, and this only applies when you have employees in the Netherlands.
   <!-- sources: src-034 (business.gov.nl — official) -->
   <!-- sources: human:aksie | flags: unverified -->

3. **[NL] Write down your sick leave notification procedure before anyone gets sick.**
   Dutch law (WVP) requires this in writing. Document: who the employee reports to and by what time on day one, what happens at the key milestones (day 6, week 6, week 42), and the role of the company doctor (bedrijfsarts). This is the employer's re-integration roadmap — missing a milestone can extend the two-year pay obligation by another year. After your first NL hire, also get insurance: if someone gets sick long-term, the two-year pay obligation can otherwise kill your runway.
   <!-- sources: src-033 (research note), src-036 (octagonpeople.com, advisory — flagged) -->
   <!-- sources: human:aksie | flags: unverified -->

4. **[NL] At 10 employees, designate a vertrouwenspersoon (confidential advisor).**
   This is legally required under Dutch law for any organisation with 10 or more employees. The role handles confidential reports of harassment, discrimination, intimidation, and aggression. Document their name, contact details, and what they handle in the handbook. For small companies, contracting an external provider is easier than an internal appointment.
   <!-- sources: src-035 (arboportaal.nl — official) -->

5. **Put all three address variants in your employee handbook: visiting address, registered KvK address, and postal address.**
   They are often different — especially if your registered address is at an accountant or flex office. Government agencies and courts use the registered address for official correspondence. Undocumented, this causes missed tax notices, UWV correspondence, and legal summons and is just a hassle.
   <!-- sources: src-033 (research note) -->

## Warning signs you're behind

### Output quality

- **[NL] You don't have hour registration in place and your company is past 3 months old.**
  Non-compliance with the Arbeidstijdenwet carries fines of up to €10,000 per employee. The Dutch Labour Inspectorate enforces this; many founders discover the obligation during an audit or dispute — by which point reconstructing historical records is difficult and the company is already exposed.
  <!-- sources: src-034 (business.gov.nl — official) -->

- **[NL] You're not tracking the WVP milestones for a sick employee.**
  Missing key milestones — problem analysis by week 6, plan of approach by week 8, UWV notification by week 42 — can trigger a loonsanctie: a mandatory extension of the two-year pay obligation. This is the most common and most avoidable financial penalty in Dutch employment law for small companies. It happens because nobody knew the timeline, not because of bad intent. An external HR service can track this for you.
  <!-- sources: src-033 (research note) -->
  <!-- sources: human:aksie | flags: unverified -->

- **You signed a traditional multi-year office lease at 2–10 people.**
  At this stage, your headcount trajectory is fundamentally uncertain. A 3–5 year lease converts a variable cost into a fixed commitment at exactly the point where optionality matters most. If you're already in one, model the sublease or exit options now rather than when you next need the runway.
  <!-- sources: src-031 (gable.to, vendor — warning corroborated across multiple sources) -->

## How this evolves next

- **Office & Facilities doesn't become a real function until around 25–50 people.**
  At First Hires it is one person's side responsibility and a handful of documents. At Early Revenue (~10–20 people), the work-location policy becomes a formal hiring criterion. At Growth (~25–50 people), facilities acquires real scope: lease management, maintenance contracts, visitor and contractor access, and formal health & safety compliance. Build that then, not now — the First Hires version is just the legal minimum and the handbook basics.
  <!-- sources: src-029 (alexrodba.com), src-030 (KVK) -->

## Tools & resources

- **Public handbook examples worth studying:** Basecamp (14 chapters, plain English, [GitHub repo](https://github.com/basecamp/handbook)) is the best model for small teams. GitLab's handbook (2,200+ pages) is the best model for thoroughness at scale — don't copy it at 10 people. Remote.com and Zapier also publish theirs and are useful for distributed team sections. Keep yours basic and assign a clear owner who will keep it up to date — small and up to date beats long and out of use.
