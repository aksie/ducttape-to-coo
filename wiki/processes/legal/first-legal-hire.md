# The first legal hire

> Cross-cutting note (relates to [4.1 Governance & Corporate](4.1--growth.md) and [4.5 Internal & Labour](4.5--growth.md)). Typically a Series A–B question.

> The first in-house lawyer usually burns out within a year — dropped into a backlog they never escape. Give them a diagnostic mandate first, keep an experienced advisor on the side, and leave routine legal in the business.

## What good looks like

- **For a new head of legal, the first period should be focused on diagnostics, not the operational firehose.**
  They work out what's actually needed — operationally and especially strategically — before being plugged in. A deliberate short mandate ("I'm only here for two months") forces that posture, because going full-time opens the floodgates.
  <!-- sources: src-062 (Trailhead first-legal-hire, human:trailhead-legal) -->

- **An experienced person sits on the side, at a distance.**
  Someone who has done this before is a sounding board to phone and a coach for a young lawyer on how to operate inside a company — the intangible "how do I operate in-house" skill is where they most need help.
  <!-- sources: src-062 (Trailhead first-legal-hire, human:trailhead-legal) -->

## What you actually need to do

1. **Keep routine legal in the business with good checklists.**
   Much day-to-day legal can be handled by the team — e.g. standard NDAs don't need an expensive lawyer (unless it's an M&A NDA). Routing every routine document through one person is what drowns the first hire and burns their strategic time.
   <!-- sources: src-062 (Trailhead first-legal-hire, human:trailhead-legal) -->

## Warning signs you're behind

### Founder / key-person time

- **The first in-house lawyer is on track to burn out within a year.**
  They inherit years of accumulated mess, drown in day-to-day work, and never get to zoom out — while the big strategic risks go unaddressed. That's both a people failure and a risk failure.
  <!-- sources: src-062 (Trailhead first-legal-hire, human:trailhead-legal) -->

- **The hire starts "3–0 down" and the founder relationship isn't managed.**
  Founders built the company, hold strong views, and perceive legal as a blocker — so the hire has to win trust back while clearing the backlog. If that relationship isn't actively managed, legal gets sidelined and the risks it was hired to catch get overruled.
  <!-- sources: src-062 (Trailhead first-legal-hire, human:trailhead-legal) -->

### Process entry

- **A lawyer left alone flags everything.**
  Trained for years to avoid risk and to say yes to additional legal work, they can make things worse when isolated — and in-house, with clients at your desk, boundary-setting is hard, personal, and needs coaching. Flagging everything erodes trust and turns legal into a bottleneck, while taking on every loosely-defined deal and turning it into contracts burns the legal hire out.
  <!-- sources: src-062 (Trailhead first-legal-hire, human:trailhead-legal) -->

## How this evolves next

- **Bringing legal fully in-house is typically a Series A/B question.**
  Before that point, episodic external help from a trusted generalist usually beats a full-time hire — hiring too early creates the burnout trap with too little strategic work to justify it. In-house makes sense once volume and strategic weight outgrow founders plus external counsel.
