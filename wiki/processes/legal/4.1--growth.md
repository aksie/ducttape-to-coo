# Governance & Corporate — Growth

> By a priced round your governance is judged on whether it works in practice, not on paper. The test is documented decisions, clear reserved matters, and an internal signing structure that takes the load off the statutory directors.

## What good looks like at this stage

- **Your articles of association reflect how governance actually works now.**
  "Up to date" is about reality, not just a document deposited at the KvK — articles that have drifted from practice create surprises in due diligence and disputes.
  <!-- sources: src-061 (Trailhead legal scan, human:trailhead-legal) -->

- **Material (important) board decisions and company direction are documented.**
  Minutes or at least an action list — in a bad-weather scenario this is what a trustee looks back on to judge whether you set course and acted in time (closing a loss-making market, who gets paid, whether to keep hiring).
  <!-- sources: src-061 (Trailhead legal scan, human:trailhead-legal) -->

- **You know your reserved matters and document their approvals.**
  The decisions that need sign-off from another body or a third party (typically an investor) are written down, and the approval is recorded when used — this is where investor consent rights bite.
  <!-- sources: src-061 (Trailhead legal scan, human:trailhead-legal) -->

- **The roles of your company's bodies are documented and governance is real in practice.**
  Board, supervisory board (RvC), advisory board (RvA), works council where present — professionalised on paper is not enough; a next round tests whether it is actually arranged.
  <!-- sources: src-061 (Trailhead legal scan, human:trailhead-legal) -->

- **There is a documented board calendar, a fixed governance rhythm, and a conflict-of-interest policy.**
  Recurring meetings and a reporting cadence keep governance proactive; ad hoc meetings only when necessary; a CoI policy pre-empts the awkward cases before they become disputes.
  <!-- sources: src-061 (Trailhead legal scan, human:trailhead-legal) -->

- **Material contracts run off lawyer-reviewed templates and sit in a central register.**
  Client and commercial contracts (suppliers, leases, IT providers) are reviewed by counsel before signing — not patched afterwards — and a register tracks parties, expiry dates, and change-of-control terms.
  <!-- sources: src-061 (Trailhead legal scan, human:trailhead-legal) -->

- **The company is appropriately insured and any litigation is tracked.**
  D&O / director liability and professional & business liability, plus cyber depending on size and activity; you can say whether any litigation is running rather than filtering on "material" — a single high-stakes settlement (VSO) can itself be material.
  <!-- sources: src-061 (Trailhead legal scan, human:trailhead-legal) -->

## What you actually need to do

1. **Keep lightweight minutes of board and management meetings.**
   An agenda with a couple of bullets per discussion point is enough — and consciously distinguish a real decision ("we are doing it") from something merely discussed ("a good idea"). Lightweight minutes make decisions traceable without bureaucracy.
   <!-- sources: src-061 (Trailhead legal scan, human:trailhead-legal) -->

2. **Set up an internal authority matrix and register a power of attorney where you need third-party effect.**
   Define who may sign what, with thresholds by area of responsibility plus a monetary amount, approved by board or shareholder resolution. Register the volmacht with the Chamber of Commerce (NL context) for externally-working representation (*derdenwerking*) — without it the delegation is internal only. This spreads accountability off the statutory directors.
   <!-- sources: src-061 (Trailhead legal scan, human:trailhead-legal) -->

3. **Map the obligations that flow from any financing and calendar the reports.**
   Read the loan terms for what must be reported and when (notification duties, covenants that trigger below certain values) and put recurring reports in the calendar. Finance owns them, but legal should know what is in there — a missed covenant becomes a default nobody saw coming.
   <!-- sources: src-061 (Trailhead legal scan, human:trailhead-legal) -->

4. **Put a trigger in place so non-standard contracts get legal review.**
   Flag contracts that exceed a monetary threshold and/or are strategically important; use change-of-control clauses as the worked example. The value is knowing which important contracts contain one and signing consciously — you often cannot renegotiate it, but you should not be surprised by it in an acquisition or financing.
   <!-- sources: src-061 (Trailhead legal scan, human:trailhead-legal) -->

## Warning signs you're behind

### Founder / key-person time

- **Everything defaults to the statutory directors, who end up blind-signing.**
  They are solely authorised by default, so signing piles onto them until they sign without reading — no real control. A next round's due diligence will flag it if an officer who is not a statutory director signs everything without a resolution authorising it.
  <!-- sources: src-061 (Trailhead legal scan, human:trailhead-legal) -->

## How this evolves next

- **Once an investor is aboard, governance gets real and consent rights bite.**
  Even with a share majority you effectively need investor approval on things like the annual budget — they can withhold it until they like the plan — so board governance, reserved matters, and reporting cadence all formalise further as you scale.
  <!-- sources: src-061 (Trailhead legal scan, human:trailhead-legal) -->

## Context variants

### If you are bootstrapped

Governance can stay lighter — there are no investor consent rights to satisfy — but the authority matrix, board/management minutes, and contract register still pay off the day you consider a trade sale or bank financing.

### If you are venture-backed (Series A and beyond)

Reserved matters and budget consent are live: even with a majority you need investor sign-off on the items in the shareholders' agreement. Expect D&O cover, a real reporting cadence, and an authority matrix that a next round's due diligence can trace back to a resolution.
