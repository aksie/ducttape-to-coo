# Legal (above certain size also Compliance) — Foundation

> At foundation, legal is about getting three things right that you can never redo cleanly: the founding documents, the IP ownership, and the filing structure. None of them take long. All of them compound badly if skipped.

## What good looks like at this stage

- **Founding documents filed and findable.** Deed of incorporation, articles of association, and a current shareholder register all live in a single known folder. The cap table — even a one-tab spreadsheet — is up to date. Any co-founder agreements (equity split, vesting, what happens if someone leaves) are signed and filed.
  <!-- sources: src-038 (Practitioner contribution — Legal & Corporate Filing Structure) -->

- **Co-founders have talked through the basics on paper — roles, capital, exit triggers — without heavyweight legal docs.** You are building and testing demand, not playing company with notaries and lawyers before a business exists. Zero paper among co-builders is as foolish as an 80-page shareholder agreement before any line of code is committed.
  <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

- **A clean entity exists and all IP — code, brand, product, content — sits in the company, including work from the pre-legal-entity period.** Execute an IP assignment from founders as part of incorporation; not "mostly," not "except the first commits."
  <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

## What you actually need to do

1. **Set up a minimal folder structure now, before anything gets lost.** Under `/Legal/` create: `/corporate/` (founding docs, cap table, shareholder register), `/contracts/` (any agreements you've signed), `/ip/` (trademarks, domains, IP assignments), `/insurance/` (when relevant). One Google Drive or Dropbox folder per section is enough. The point is that documents have a home — not that the home is elaborate. When you want the full map across legal, finance, HR, and compliance, use the [Company Ops Registry](../../../templates/rendered/company-ops-registry.html) template.
   <!-- sources: src-038 (Practitioner contribution — Legal & Corporate Filing Structure) -->

2. **Register trademarks and domain names early, and record their renewal dates immediately.** Don't wait until you're bigger. Trademark and domain registrations lapse silently — there are no urgent reminders. Note renewal dates in the folder or a simple spreadsheet at the moment you register. A lapsed trademark or domain name is recoverable only slowly and sometimes not at all.
   <!-- sources: src-038 (Practitioner contribution — Legal & Corporate Filing Structure) -->

3. **Have every founder assign their IP to the company in writing as soon as possible.** This is a standard clause in a co-founder agreement or a separate IP assignment document. Without it, the IP legally belongs to the individual founder, not the company — which surfaces as a material finding in every future due diligence.
   <!-- sources: editorial | flags: unverified -->

4. **Block an evening to agree co-founder scenarios and commit to a formalisation trigger.** Roles, money in, what happens if it fails — then explicitly: once we see traction, we incorporate, transfer IP, and document founder terms.
   <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

5. **Incorporate when you have enough effort put in — if you have put several man-months in, it is worth it to formalise things — or if you are close to real momentum: users, revenue, or serious investor or partner interest — not on day one of the idea.** Escalate legal work in step with real risk (traction, people, money, data), not founder anxiety.
   <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

6. **Keep your corporate structure simple — standard template (in NL: a BV) setup, no exotic holdings from networking advice.** Complexity is where expensive fixes live; advisors regularly see founders locked into arrangements they do not understand.
   <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

7. **Once a quarter, block an hour to review your operational status, perhaps with an experienced operator or friend.** Look at employment papers, customer/supplier contracts, privacy, tech/data architecture, and tax/compliance. A fixed rhythm replaces oscillating between "we have nothing" panic and "we don't need this" denial.
   <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

## Warning signs you're behind

### Output quality

- **A disorganised data room causes disproportionate damage.** When investors or buyers review your company — even informally — the organisation of your documents signals how you run operations overall. A poorly structured data room makes them more critical of everything that follows, even if the underlying business is sound. This applies from your first angel round onwards.
  <!-- sources: src-038 (Practitioner contribution — Legal & Corporate Filing Structure) -->

### Founder / key-person time

- **You commissioned extensive founder legal work or spend disproportionate time with notaries and lawyers before you are close to a beta version of the product.** Legal structure is eating energy that should go to product and potential customers — you are timmering everything shut before learning if the idea works.
  <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

- **You swing between blind panic about legal gaps and blanket denial that documentation is needed — with no structured check-in.** Unstructured emotional response leads to wasted spend or due-diligence surprises.
  <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

### Process entry

- **Two co-founders are building together with no written understanding of roles, capital contribution, or exit triggers.** Verbal-only deals become expensive once money, IP, or investors appear.
  <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

- **After incorporation, meaningful IP, code, or brand assets still sit with individual founders.** Do not assume "IP never killed a deal" — this drives unclean cap tables and due-diligence pain.
  <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

- **Your structure includes exotic arrangements one founder adopted from informal advice without understanding them.** These become expensive to explain, fix, or defend in diligence.
  <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

## How this evolves next

- **At First Hires, compliance obligations begin — and you need a named owner for the HR admin work before documents start defaulting to email.** Once you have employees, Dutch employment law activates (hour registration, sick leave procedures, vertrouwenspersoon). Designate one person responsible for the filing structure before the first hire.
  <!-- sources: src-038 (Practitioner contribution — Legal & Corporate Filing Structure) -->

## Context variants

### If you are bootstrapped

The filing structure applies regardless of whether you plan to raise external capital. If you ever sell the company, bring in a co-investor, or face a legal dispute, your documents will need to be found fast. Set it up once and maintain it.

### If you have angel or friends & family investors

You have already opened your documents informally to external parties. Make sure what they saw was properly filed. Before your next round, verify that the shareholder register reflects reality and that all signed agreements are on file.

### If you are venture-backed (seed / Series A)

This is the highest-stakes scenario for data room quality. Investors at seed/Series A conduct structured due diligence and form a lasting first impression from how your documents are organised. Consider a brief review with external legal counsel before opening the data room — not after.
