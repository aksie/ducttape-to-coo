# Governance & Corporate — Early Revenue

> By early revenue, the legal filing structure you've been building either exists and costs you almost nothing to maintain — or it doesn't, and you'll spend weeks reconstructing it at the worst possible moment. There is no neutral state.

## What good looks like at this stage

- **The filing structure is complete and navigable by someone who doesn't know the company.** Annual accounts are filed and stored. Tax filings are current. The top contracts are listed with one-liners. The compliance calendar tells you what's due and when. A new CFO or external counsel could orient themselves within a day.
  <!-- sources: src-038 (Practitioner contribution — Legal & Corporate Filing Structure) -->

- **Before first outside money, you have documented founder/shareholder arrangements at template level, a clear cap table, and IP already in the entity.** The moment you give away equity, a third party with real money expects professionalisation — not "we have nothing on paper."
  <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

- **Annual accounts are properly adopted and filed on time.** Adoption is by a shareholders' resolution signed by all shareholders, the accounts are signed by the board, and at minimum the CFO knows the filing status. Doing this correctly and on time is what keeps the filings defensible later.
  <!-- sources: src-061 (Trailhead legal scan, human:trailhead-legal) -->

## What you actually need to do

1. **Maintain a compliance calendar — a single spreadsheet or set of recurring reminders.** Include: annual accounts deadline (within 5 months of financial year end for a Dutch BV), corporate income tax (VPB), KvK annual filing, VAT/BTW frequency, insurance renewal dates, trademark renewal dates, domain renewal dates, WBSO application windows (twice yearly), works council consultation obligations if applicable. None of these send urgent reminders — missing them has consequences. Do this once a year, set a named owner, and invite a second founder and your external financial admin or accountant to review it together.
   <!-- sources: src-038 (Practitioner contribution — Legal & Corporate Filing Structure) -->

2. **Have an external legal advisor walk through your full filing once before you approach investors or buyers.** Even if you have M&A experience yourself, self-assessment of your own DD readiness is unreliable. You know the context behind every document and unconsciously overlook gaps that an external reviewer will catch. Common findings: missing addenda confirming salary changes, contracts not yet countersigned, documents filed inconsistently.
   <!-- sources: src-038 (Practitioner contribution — Legal & Corporate Filing Structure) -->

3. **At friends, family, or first angels, use a sensible template for founder/shareholder terms that documents your principles and non-negotiables.** In the Netherlands, [Capital Waters](https://capitalwaters.nl/investment-documents/) offers open-source ordinary share agreements, convertible loans (CLA), and EPOS (Dutch SAFE equivalent) — pick the instrument that matches the deal, adapt rather than start from zero. Do not pay premium bespoke legal fees for a full package if a professional round is likely soon — the next investor will often want their own documents. Do get IP in and key principles on paper.
   <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

4. **Ensure written terms exist with customers, suppliers, and key technology vendors — not only internal employment papers.** Handshakes with friends-and-family money, hires, suppliers, and customers are a common failure mode once scale and scrutiny increase.
   <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

## Warning signs you're behind

### Output quality

- **Adoption of the annual accounts is treated as "filing Thursday, assume approval unless I hear otherwise."** That email is not valid adoption in most shareholder agreements — and late or incorrect filing of annual accounts can trigger director liability. A trustee in an insolvency looks back at exactly these filings.
  <!-- sources: src-061 (Trailhead legal scan, human:trailhead-legal) -->

- **Filing gaps found by an investor's or buyer's legal team will damage your negotiating position.** The gaps don't have to be serious. Disorganisation signals risk — and buyers price risk. The most likely outcomes are a lower price or a longer list of representations and warranties. The cost of one week of remediation before opening a data room is a fraction of what those gaps cost at the negotiating table.
  <!-- sources: src-038 (Practitioner contribution — Legal & Corporate Filing Structure) -->

- **Self-assessment of DD readiness is unreliable, even for experienced operators.** Even operators with M&A experience on the buy side have found a solid week of gaps when reviewing with an external advisor before a fundraise. The closer you are to the business, the more context you unconsciously fill in for missing or inconsistent documentation.
  <!-- sources: src-038 (Practitioner contribution — Legal & Corporate Filing Structure) -->

- **Privacy & data-architecture diligence-readiness → [4.6 Privacy & IP — Early Revenue](4.6--early-revenue.md).**

### Process entry

- **You are treating a first external investor like an internal co-founder conversation, or skipping documentation because they are friends or family.** Outside money changes the relationship; undocumented terms create misalignment and cap-table explanations that hurt follow-on fundraising.
  <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

- **Key commercial relationships still run on handshakes — or leadership's management agreements are still missing years into operations.** Informal deals work until they do not; retrofitting is slow and relationship-sensitive.
  <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

## How this evolves next

- **At Growth, the compliance calendar and filing structure need to be complete enough to hand off.** A new CFO or legal counsel should be able to get fully oriented within a day without asking anyone questions. The transaction-prep section of the filing structure activates — not for year-round maintenance, but as the place to quickly populate when a deal is live.
  <!-- sources: src-038 (Practitioner contribution — Legal & Corporate Filing Structure) -->

- **Approaching a professional funding round, legal ops must be transaction-ready: consistent contracts, privacy and tech that survive due diligence, filing that does not require reconstruction.** Institutional investors run systematic diligence; informal early-stage habits must be professionalised before term sheet, not during it.
  <!-- sources: src-041 (startup legal interview, human:stefanverkerk) -->

## Context variants

### If you are bootstrapped

Even without immediate investor pressure, the filing discipline matters if you ever consider a trade sale. A data room assembled under time pressure for a potential buyer is both stressful and expensive. The compliance calendar costs almost nothing to maintain year-round.

### If you have angel or friends & family investors

You are likely in or approaching a formal fundraising process. The external legal advisor review (item 2 above) is especially valuable at this stage — your angels may not have done rigorous due diligence, but your Series A investors will. For NL deals, document the round with [Capital Waters](https://capitalwaters.nl/investment-documents/) CLA or EPOS templates if you are not doing a priced equity round yet; use ordinary share templates if you are. Keep the cap table in your filing structure using their Excel template.

### If you are venture-backed (seed / Series A)

Your investors' legal team will conduct structured due diligence. IP assignments, employment contract consistency, and the completeness of the corporate filing are all reviewed. One week of external counsel review before opening the data room is a standard investment that consistently pays off. For NL priced rounds, [Capital Waters preferred share](https://capitalwaters.nl/investment-documents/) subscription, shareholders agreement, and term sheet templates are a sensible starting point before counsel customises for your round.

## Tools & resources

- **Compliance calendar:** a shared Google Sheet with one tab per year is enough. Assign an owner and schedule an annual review.
- **Filing structure:** Google Drive or SharePoint — no dedicated legal tool required at this stage. Use the [Company Ops Registry](../../../templates/rendered/company-ops-registry.html) for folder layout.
- **[Capital Waters](https://capitalwaters.nl/investment-documents/)** — open-source NL investment document templates: cap table (Excel), ordinary/preferred share agreements, CLA, EPOS. Comply-or-explain license; get counsel before signing.
- Arboportaal.nl (NL employment law summary and compliance checklists)
