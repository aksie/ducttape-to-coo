# Accounts Payable & Expense Management — First Hires

> At First Hires, expense management is barely a process — but the habits you build now determine whether your books are clean or chaotic when it starts to matter.

## What good looks like at this stage

You have a clear payment approval threshold: everyone knows what they can spend without asking, and anything above that amount gets a second pair of eyes before it's paid.
<!-- sources: src-008 (founderpath, sells_fractional_cfo_services) -->

You have an accountant engaged — not necessarily full-time, but someone you can call with questions like "Can our cash flow support a new hire?" or "How much should we set aside for taxes this quarter?" When you are in doubt yourself. They should work with businesses similar to yours and should be able to walk you through the reporting so you would be able to answer it yourself, and they are just an experienced extra set of eyes in your decision. Because you need someone specialised to improve the quality of what you could do yourself, but you need to understand it yourself to be able to run the business.
<!-- sources: src-017 (ycombinator) -->
<!-- sources: human:aksie | flags: unverified -->

## What you actually need to do

1. Move all business expenses onto one or a small set of dedicated cards. Even smoother for the company is when founders / employees pay expenses (anything except SaaS stuff) on their personal card, and only get reimbursed if they have the receipt. Because the cost of admin from a $20 coffee receipt with a potential customer is much higher than $7. The rule is that no personal expenses mix in. Because at founder marginal tax rates (50%+), every missed deductible expense costs roughly half its value.
<!-- sources: src-017 (ycombinator) -->
<!-- sources: human:aksie | flags: practitioner_rewrite -->

2. Track every expense from day one, and when your spending is at the size where someone in the founding team spends more than an hour a month to brief the finance admin on what cost category it is, structure the expense categories because retroactive recategorisation is brutal and you will hate yourself later if you don't.
<!-- sources: src-019 (zeni, sells_finance_concierge_service), src-005 (ycombinator) -->
<!-- sources: human:aksie | flags: practitioner_rewrite -->

3. Get a bookkeeper earlier than you think you need one. They'll pay for themselves within 3 months in founder time saved — your job is to build the company, not categorise transactions.
<!-- sources: src-006 (reddit) -->

4. Document your expense policy, at a certain size add it to a handbook that people "sign off on", but keep the tone in line with your size. A verbal or undocumented or implied policy doesn't survive growth past 15 people and fails diligence every time. Because at first "just follow common sense and spend like it is your own money" will work, but people are different and have started at the company at different stages, so will not automatically take the decisions you think make sense.
<!-- sources: src-008 (founderpath, sells_fractional_cfo_services) -->
<!-- sources: human:aksie | flags: unverified -->

5. Have your external accountant match every card transaction to a receipt and a category before closing the month. Because with proper tooling this takes less than 2 hours; if you skip it, uncategorised transactions compound into a mess.
<!-- sources: src-009 (bench, accounting_firm_interest), src-008 (founderpath, sells_fractional_cfo_services) -->
<!-- sources: human:aksie | flags: unverified -->

## Warning signs you're behind

- You can't quickly produce a receipt for any business expense the tax authority (IRS in the US) might ask about. If you can't find a specific receipt on demand, you'll lose the deduction plus pay penalties.
<!-- sources: src-017 (ycombinator) -->

- Multiple people are charging expenses on a shared company card with no per-person visibility. You find out what was spent at the end of the month, after the money is already gone.
<!-- sources: src-018 (spendesk, sells_expense_management_software) -->

## How this evolves next

At Early Scale (~15-25 people), expense management becomes a real process: you'll need defined approval workflows (who approves what amount), the volume will be too high to deal with finding receipts in hindsight. Either your quality will be too low and create a tax risk, or you will be spending too much time on it to fix things in hindsight.
<!-- sources: human:aksie | flags: practitioner_rewrite -->

---

## Tools & resources

- A dedicated business credit card (or small set of cards) — no personal expense mixing
- A simple expense log or spreadsheet if you're pre-tooling; Pleo, Spendesk, or Ramp once the team grows
- A bookkeeper (part-time or outsourced) engaged before you think you need one
- A one-page written expense policy: thresholds, categories, receipt requirements
