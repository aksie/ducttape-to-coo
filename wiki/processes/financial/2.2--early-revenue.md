# Financial Planning, Budgeting & Analysis — Early Revenue

> At early scale, financial operations shifts from survival-mode cash tracking to a repeatable system that gives you and your team real visibility. The goal is not sophisticated finance — it's reliable, timely numbers that inform decisions.

## What good looks like at this stage

You have a live financial model that you update and review regularly — not a document that exists only for fundraising decks. So that you are not needing to build a model when you do go into fundraising mode, that is why you should have a model that is up to date every month. And you should review and use it so you know about your financial headlines: run rate, and things to change when run rate becomes too short.
<!-- sources: src-002 (lennysnewsletter, vc_portfolio_interest, series_a_plus_bias) -->

Your monthly close is completed within 5 business days of month end, every month. The books are never more than 4 weeks behind. This is a little strong for the current phase, but you are scaling — so it will be needed for your next step of scale, and gearing up while you are already behind is twice the work.
<!-- sources: src-001 (getexact, sells_fractional_cfo_services), src-005 (ycombinator, practitioner) -->

Cash position and months of runway are visible to the full leadership team, not just the CEO. Everyone making spending decisions understands the constraints. Weekly visibility means the whole leadership team can take decisions with the same information — not one person having all the weight on their shoulders — and it makes the quality of decisions better.
<!-- sources: src-007 (visible_vc, sells_software), src-005 (ycombinator, practitioner) -->

COGS and operating expenses are separated in your chart of accounts, giving you a clean gross margin line without analytical overhead. Not in month one of revenue — that would be too much financial setup for your stage — but once you have a repeatable revenue model, get this in place. Retroactive separation is a significant analytical burden; it's worth doing imperfectly now rather than perfectly later.
<!-- sources: src-005 (ycombinator, practitioner), src-009 (bench, accounting_firm_interest) -->

Your accounting software integrates with or feeds directly into the spreadsheet or tool you use for forecasting, so you can keep actuals and forecast aligned without significant manual work. If you have to manually copy numbers between systems every month, you will stop doing it.
<!-- sources: human:aksie | flags: unverified -->

## What you actually need to do

1. Do a monthly variance review — actual vs. forecast — and write a short explanation for any line that missed by more than 10%. The explanation matters more than the number. This is important because it will make you understand where your business is highly variable, or where your forecast isn't that good yet.
<!-- sources: src-002 (lennysnewsletter, vc_portfolio_interest), src-006 (reddit, practitioner) -->

2. Run a monthly P&L review with the founding team, but in a P&L format that everybody understands. Simplify what comes out of accounting so it makes sense for all and people know how it should affect their decisions. Put it in the calendar. Make it a standing meeting, not an ad hoc one.
<!-- sources: src-006 (reddit, practitioner) -->

3. Document your expense policy. At a certain size, add it to a handbook that people sign off on — but keep the tone in line with your size. A verbal or undocumented policy doesn't survive growth past 15 people and fails diligence every time. At first "just follow common sense and spend like it's your own money" will work, but people are different and have joined at different stages, so they will not automatically make the decisions you think make sense.
<!-- sources: src-008 (founderpath, sells_fractional_cfo_services, series_a_plus_bias) | flags: missing_why -->

## Warning signs you're behind

- Your books are more than 4 weeks behind and you're making spending or hiring decisions based on bank balance.
<!-- sources: src-005 (ycombinator, practitioner), src-009 (bench, accounting_firm_interest) -->

- You haven't done a budget vs. actuals review, or the first time you tried, you couldn't make sense of the categories because they've been applied inconsistently.
<!-- sources: src-004 (cfodive, news), src-006 (reddit, practitioner) -->

- Your expense categories are inconsistent across months, and cost levels go up and down without you understanding what caused it. This makes historical data unreliable for forecasting.
<!-- sources: src-004 (cfodive, news) -->

- You treat the bank balance as a proxy for financial health. If you've ever been surprised that cash was fine but other dynamics were bad — the business was actually losing money, or margins might not be enough — this is the symptom.
<!-- sources: src-009 (bench, accounting_firm_interest), src-004 (cfodive, news) | flags: too_generic -->

## How this evolves next

At Growth stage, financial operations typically shifts from founder-managed or outsourced to a dedicated internal finance function. The trigger is usually Series A close or reaching 30-40 people, whichever comes first — below this threshold, fractional or outsourced finance is more cost-effective; above it, the coordination overhead starts to outweigh the cost of a full-time hire.
<!-- sources: src-001 (getexact, sells_fractional_cfo_services) | flags: too_generic, missing_why -->

Department heads begin owning their own budget lines. Centralised budget management doesn't scale past 40-50 people — you have too many cost centres for one person to manage effectively. The finance function shifts from doing the work to enabling accountability and providing visibility.
<!-- sources: src-003 (a16z, vc_portfolio_interest, series_a_plus_bias) | flags: missing_why -->

## Context variants

### If you are bootstrapped

Cash visibility is existential rather than strategic. Review cash position weekly rather than monthly — monthly cadence gives you too little lead time to act when runway is under 18 months. The P&L review matters just as much, but your focus is burn rate and runway above all else.

### If you have angel or friends & family investors

Light reporting obligations, but start the habit of a monthly written update now. The discipline of writing it forces you to look at the numbers. Your investors will appreciate it, and it prepares you for the more structured reporting a later round will require.

### If you are venture-backed (seed / Series A)

Board reporting cadence should be established before your Series A closes, not after. Build a consistent board pack template: actuals, forecast, key metrics, and narrative. The finance function exists partly to serve investor communication — factor that into the monthly rhythm.

### If you are venture-backed (growth / PE)

The internal finance hire is likely already in place or imminent. Focus shifts to FP&A rigour: quarterly reforecasting, scenario planning, and department-level budget ownership. Board packs become more formal and data-intensive.

## Tools & resources

- **Financial model:** A shared Google Sheet updated monthly is sufficient at this stage. Complexity is the enemy of consistency — the best model is the one that actually gets updated.
- **Accounting software:** QuickBooks, Xero, or equivalent. Set up correctly from the start; migrations are painful. Check that it can connect to your forecasting tool before committing.
- **Bookkeeping:** Bench, Pilot, or a local bookkeeper. Key criterion: API or automation compatibility so it scales later.
- **Investor reporting (if applicable):** A consistent Google Slides template beats a fancy tool. Consistency matters more than the medium.
