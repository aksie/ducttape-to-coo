# Financial Planning, Budgeting & Analysis — Early Revenue

> At early scale, financial operations shifts from survival-mode cash tracking to a repeatable system that gives you and your team real visibility. The goal is not sophisticated finance — it's reliable, timely numbers that inform decisions.

## What good looks like at this stage

- **A live financial model you actually use — not a fundraising artifact.**
  Update and review it regularly so you're not scrambling to build one when fundraising starts. Review it monthly so you know your run rate and can spot when things need to change.
  <!-- sources: src-002 (lennysnewsletter, vc_portfolio_interest, series_a_plus_bias) -->

- **Monthly close completed within 5 business days of month end.**
  The books are never more than 4 weeks behind. This is a little strong for the current phase, but you are scaling — gearing up while already behind is twice the work.
  <!-- sources: src-001 (getexact, sells_fractional_cfo_services), src-005 (ycombinator, practitioner) -->

- **Cash position and runway visible to the full leadership team.**
  Not just the CEO. Everyone making spending decisions understands the constraints. Weekly visibility means better decisions — and no single person carrying all the weight.
  <!-- sources: src-007 (visible_vc, sells_software), src-005 (ycombinator, practitioner) -->

- **COGS and operating expenses separated in your chart of accounts.**
  Not in month one of revenue — that's too much setup for your stage. But once you have a repeatable revenue model, get this in place. Retroactive separation is a significant analytical burden; it's worth doing imperfectly now rather than perfectly later.
  <!-- sources: src-005 (ycombinator, practitioner), src-009 (bench, accounting_firm_interest) -->

- **Accounting software feeds into your forecasting tool.**
  If you have to manually copy numbers between systems every month, you will stop doing it.
  <!-- sources: human:aksie | flags: unverified -->

## What you actually need to do

- **Run a monthly variance review: actual vs. forecast.**
  Write a short explanation for any line that missed by more than 10%. The explanation matters more than the number — it makes you understand where your business is highly variable or where your forecast isn't good yet.
  <!-- sources: src-002 (lennysnewsletter, vc_portfolio_interest), src-006 (reddit, practitioner) -->

- **Hold a monthly P&L review with the founding team.**
  Put it in a format everybody understands — simplify what comes out of accounting so people know how it affects their decisions. Make it a standing calendar meeting, not ad hoc.
  <!-- sources: src-006 (reddit, practitioner) -->

- **Document your expense policy in writing.**
  At a certain size, add it to a handbook that people sign off on — but keep the tone in line with your size. "Just follow common sense and spend like it's your own money" works at first, but people are different and join at different stages. A verbal policy doesn't survive growth past 15 people and fails diligence every time.
  <!-- sources: src-008 (founderpath, sells_fractional_cfo_services, series_a_plus_bias) | flags: missing_why -->

## Warning signs you're behind

### Output quality

- **You treat the bank balance as a proxy for financial health.**
  If you've ever been surprised that cash was fine but the business was actually losing money, or margins weren't enough — this is the symptom. Bank balance and P&L diverge in opposite directions depending on invoicing timing.
  <!-- sources: src-009 (bench, accounting_firm_interest), src-004 (cfodive, news) | flags: too_generic -->

- **Expense categories are inconsistent across months.**
  Cost levels go up and down without you understanding what caused it. Looking forward, this makes historical data unreliable for forecasting.
  <!-- sources: src-004 (cfodive, news) -->

### Founder / key-person time

- **Books are more than 4 weeks behind.**
  You're making spending or hiring decisions based on bank balance because you don't have anything better. Every decision made on stale data carries hidden risk.
  <!-- sources: src-005 (ycombinator, practitioner), src-009 (bench, accounting_firm_interest) -->

- **First budget vs. actuals review was incomprehensible.**
  You haven't done one, or the first time you tried, you couldn't make sense of the categories because they've been applied inconsistently.
  <!-- sources: src-004 (cfodive, news), src-006 (reddit, practitioner) -->

## How this evolves next

- **At Growth, finance shifts from founder-managed to a dedicated function.**
  The trigger is usually Series A close or reaching 30-40 people. Below that, fractional or outsourced finance is more cost-effective; above it, coordination overhead outweighs the cost of a full-time hire.
  <!-- sources: src-001 (getexact, sells_fractional_cfo_services) | flags: too_generic, missing_why -->

- **Department heads begin owning their own budget lines.**
  Centralised budget management doesn't scale past 40-50 people. The finance function shifts from doing the work to enabling accountability and providing visibility.
  <!-- sources: src-003 (a16z, vc_portfolio_interest, series_a_plus_bias) | flags: missing_why -->

## Context variants

### If you are bootstrapped

Cash visibility is existential rather than strategic. Review cash position weekly rather than monthly — monthly cadence gives you too little lead time to act when runway is under 18 months. The P&L review matters just as much, but your focus is burn rate and runway above all else.

### If you have angel or friends & family investors

Light reporting obligations, but start the habit of a monthly written update now. The discipline of writing it forces you to look at the numbers. Your investors will appreciate it, and it prepares you for the more structured reporting a later round will require.

### If you are venture-backed (seed / Series A)

Board reporting cadence should be established before your Series A closes, not after. Build a consistent board pack template: actuals, forecast, key metrics, and narrative. The finance function exists partly to serve investor communication — factor that into the monthly rhythm.

### If you are venture-backed (growth / PE)

The internal finance hire is likely already in place or imminent. Focus shifts to FP&A rigour: quarterly reforecasting, scenario planning, and department-level budget ownership. Board packs become more formal and data-intensive.

## Tools & resources

- **Financial model:** A shared Google Sheet updated monthly is sufficient. Complexity is the enemy of consistency — the best model is the one that actually gets updated.
- **Accounting software:** QuickBooks, Xero, or equivalent. Set up correctly from the start; migrations are painful. Check that it can connect to your forecasting tool before committing.
- **Bookkeeping:** Bench, Pilot, or a local bookkeeper. Key criterion: API or automation compatibility so it scales later.
- **Investor reporting (if applicable):** A consistent Google Slides template beats a fancy tool. Consistency matters more than the medium.
