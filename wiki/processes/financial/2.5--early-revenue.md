# Financial Administration — Early Revenue

> At early scale, financial operations shifts from survival-mode cash tracking to a repeatable system that gives you and your team real visibility. The goal is not sophisticated finance — it's reliable, timely numbers that inform decisions.

## What good looks like at this stage

- **You have a live financial model that you update and review regularly — not a document that exists only for fundraising decks.** Maintain it so you are not building a model from scratch when you enter fundraising mode. Review and use it monthly so you know your financial headlines (run rate, and what to change when runway gets short).
  <!-- sources: src-002 (The financial model every startup needs, lennysnewsletter) | flags: vc_portfolio_interest, series_a_plus_bias -->

- **Your monthly close is completed within 5 business days of month end, every month. The books are never more than 4 weeks behind.** This is demanding at your current phase, but you are scaling — gearing up while you are already behind is twice the work.
  <!-- sources: src-001 (CFO for Startups, getexact), src-005 (Ask HN: finance processes) | flags: sells_fractional_cfo_services, accounting_firm_interest -->

- **Cash position and months of runway are visible to the full leadership team, not just the CEO.** Everyone making spending decisions understands the constraints. Monthly cadence gives enough lead time to act; shared visibility spreads strategic finance decisions across leadership instead of one person carrying all the weight.
  <!-- sources: src-007 (Startup Financial Reporting, visible.vc) | flags: sells_software, vc_portfolio_interest -->

- **COGS and operating expenses are separated in your chart of accounts, giving you a clean gross margin line without analytical overhead.** Not in month one of revenue — that would be too much financial setup for your stage — but get something in place before retroactive separation becomes a burden.
  <!-- sources: src-005 (Ask HN: finance processes) -->

## What you actually need to do

1. **Do a monthly variance review — actual vs. forecast — and write a short explanation for any line that missed by more than 10%.** The explanation matters more than the number. This helps you see where the business is highly variable (or where the forecast is not that good).
   <!-- sources: src-002 (The financial model every startup needs, lennysnewsletter) | flags: vc_portfolio_interest, series_a_plus_bias -->

2. **Run a monthly P&L review with the founding team in a format everyone understands.** Simplify what comes out of accounting so it maps to decisions people can act on. Put it in the calendar. Make it a standing meeting, not an ad hoc one.
   <!-- sources: src-006 (What I learned running finance at a 20-person startup, reddit) -->

3. **Document your expense policy; at a certain size add it to a handbook people sign off on, but keep the tone in line with your size.** A verbal, undocumented, or implied policy does not survive growth past 15 people and fails diligence every time. At first, "spend like it's your own money" can work — but people are different and joined at different stages, so they will not automatically make the spending calls you expect.
   <!-- sources: src-008 (Financial Controls Every Startup Needs Before Series A, founderpath) | flags: sells_fractional_cfo_services, series_a_plus_bias -->
   <!-- sources: human:aksie | flags: practitioner_rewrite -->

## Warning signs you're behind

### Output quality

- **Your books are more than 4 weeks behind and you're making spending or hiring decisions based on bank balance.**
  <!-- sources: src-005 (Ask HN: finance processes), src-009 (Startup Bookkeeping, bench) | flags: sells_fractional_cfo_services, accounting_firm_interest -->

- **Your expense categories are inconsistent across months, and cost levels go up and down without you understanding what caused it.** Looking forward, historical data becomes unreliable for forecasting.
  <!-- sources: src-004 (The most common finance mistakes startups make, cfodive) -->

- **You treat the bank balance as a proxy for financial health.** If you have ever been surprised that cash was fine but other dynamics were bad — the business was actually losing money (or vice versa), or margins might not be enough — this is the symptom.
  <!-- sources: src-004 (The most common finance mistakes startups make, cfodive), src-009 (Startup Bookkeeping, bench) | flags: sells_fractional_cfo_services, accounting_firm_interest -->

### Process entry

- **You haven't done a budget vs. actuals review, or the first time you tried, you couldn't make sense of the categories because they've been applied inconsistently.**
  <!-- sources: src-004 (The most common finance mistakes startups make, cfodive), src-006 (What I learned running finance at a 20-person startup, reddit) -->

## How this evolves next

- **At Growth stage, financial operations shifts from founder-managed or outsourced to a dedicated internal finance function — typically triggered by Series A close or reaching 30–40 people, whichever comes first.** Below that threshold, fractional or outsourced finance is usually more cost-effective; above it, coordination overhead often costs more than a full-time hire.
  <!-- sources: src-001 (CFO for Startups, getexact) | flags: sells_fractional_cfo_services, accounting_firm_interest -->

- **Department heads begin owning their own budget lines.** The finance function shifts from doing the work to enabling accountability and providing visibility. By 40–50 people, one person cannot manage every cost centre effectively — distributed ownership with central visibility is the mature model.
  <!-- sources: src-003 (Building the Finance Function, a16z) | flags: vc_portfolio_interest, series_a_plus_bias -->

## Tools & resources

- **Cash forecast:** A simple shared spreadsheet reviewed weekly is sufficient — complexity is the enemy of consistency at this stage.
- **Bookkeeping:** Bench, Pilot, or a local bookkeeper. The key criterion is API/automation compatibility so it scales later.
- **Investor reporting (if applicable):** Visible.vc, Briefcase, or a consistent Google Slides template. Consistency matters more than the tool.
- **Accounting software:** QuickBooks, Xero, or equivalent. Set up correctly from the start; migrations are painful.
