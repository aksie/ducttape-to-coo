# Financial Planning, Budgeting & Analysis — First Hires

> At first hires, planning is not three parallel scenarios in a deck — it is **one active budget** plus a **trigger → action list** your team can execute under stress. Calendar hire dates are forecasts; thresholds are the gates.

## What good looks like at this stage

- **Two layers, not one spreadsheet pretending the future is fixed.**
  A base plan (headcount, burn, runway) plus pre-agreed scenario rules: when a metric crosses a line, you switch to the budget you already agreed for what happens next — not a second parallel plan debated from scratch. You run one active budget today and a list of if/then rules across MRR, customer count, geography, and runway — not base/bull/bear scenarios maintained all year.
  <!-- sources: src-065 (practitioner contribution, stefanverkerk + Nalden IFTTT framing) -->

- **Every planned hire has a trigger, not just a calendar date.**
  Example: hire the next developer when ARR passes a threshold and the hire still leaves runway above a floor — the month on the sheet is a forecast, not a locked gate.
  <!-- sources: src-065 (practitioner contribution) -->

- **Material spend has pre-agreed responses when a named warning fires.**
  Not a vague "we'll tighten up" without owners or ordered actions. Founders already argued the downside in a calm month; execution in a bad month is follow-the-sheet, not a fresh crisis negotiation.
  <!-- sources: src-065 (practitioner contribution) -->

- **Leadership knows which numbers are watched and what happens at each threshold.**
  No surprise "we're cutting tomorrow" without context — the annual board budget may be a governance guardrail, but operating decisions are conditional (when unit economics work), not fixed in October.
  <!-- sources: src-065 (practitioner contribution) -->

## What you actually need to do

- **Build a budget spreadsheet — simplified P&L first, headcount detail second.**
  **Main tab:** ledger lines as rows, months as columns — a simplified version of the P&L in your accounting software (revenue, COGS if you have it, major opex lines, payroll, the rest). Roll it down through **EBITDA → EBT → cash → cumulative cash**. Start with educated guesses; each month paste or map your accountant's actual P&L into the same rows so plan vs actual lives in one place. **People tab** (or a payroll section): role, start month, base cost, fully loaded multiplier (~1.25–1.35× for NL/EU unless your accountant gives a better number) — loaded headcount costs feed the payroll line on the main tab. Run a monthly founder review (15–30 min): actual vs plan, which triggers are close or have fired, next hire gate status. For weekly runway when cash is tight, see [2.1 Cash Flow](../financial/2.1--first-hires.md) — keep both sheets aligned.
  <!-- sources: src-065 (practitioner contribution) -->
  <!-- sources: human:aksie | flags: unverified -->

- **Maintain a trigger → action table** (if this | then that — no scenario-name column).
  Review it monthly; when a line crosses, execute that day — no renegotiation in the moment. The action is usually a budget change from that moment forward.
  <!-- sources: src-065 (practitioner contribution) -->

- **Write the list with your co-founder** (and board or lead investor if you have one) **before you need it.**
  Decide in calm weather; Corona-style shocks are one hard row on the same list, not a separate ritual.
  <!-- sources: src-065 (practitioner contribution) -->

- **Add rows for your business.**
  Examples that fit the pattern: MRR ≥ threshold for two months → hire next developer and refresh loaded cost; paying customers exceed N with sustained support load → hire support; MRR down >15% MoM → freeze net-new hires, cut freelancers by agreed %, defer discretionary spend; runway < 6 months with no path back in 90 days → start fundraise prep (data room, narrative, investor list). Threshold numbers are illustrative — set yours with your model.
  <!-- sources: src-065 (practitioner contribution) | flags: needs_practitioner_check -->

- **Optionally: pre-write one row for a nasty shock you can name** — mass churn, lost anchor customer, delayed fundraise: trigger, ordered actions, owners.
  You do not need a library of disasters; you need the list from the trigger table with at least one downside row stress-tested.
  <!-- sources: src-065 (practitioner contribution) -->

## Warning signs you're behind

### Process entry

- **Static budget only — hire dates on the calendar with no link to revenue or triggers.**
  Every hire debate is moral ("we need this person") not arithmetic ("trigger met or not").
  <!-- sources: src-065 (practitioner contribution) -->

- **Triggers live only in founders' heads.**
  You "know" you'll cut freelancers, but disagree on when the moment hits.
  <!-- sources: src-065 (practitioner contribution) -->

- **Budget vs actual without clean expense categories** — variance review is useless.
  Fix categories in [2.4 AP & Expense Management](../financial/2.4--first-hires.md) first.
  <!-- sources: src-065 (practitioner contribution) -->

- **Last cuts conversation was after something broke** — reactive whiplash, not follow-the-sheet.
  <!-- sources: src-065 (practitioner contribution) -->

### Output quality

- **Optimistic revenue in the base case — downside never named.**
  Survival math at zero revenue is missing; the plan only works if the good month keeps coming.
  <!-- sources: src-065 (practitioner contribution) -->

### Founder / key-person time

- **Only the CEO holds the numbers.**
  Everyone else optimizes locally while burn compounds collectively.
  <!-- sources: src-065 (practitioner contribution) -->

- **You can't answer "what happens to cash if we hire X next month?" within a working day.**
  <!-- sources: src-065 (practitioner contribution) -->

## How this evolves next

- **At Early Revenue, add more trigger lines and a rolling cash view.**
  Your investor update scenario section should mirror the internal sheet — it does not replace it. See [1.2b Shareholder & Investor Reporting](../strategic/1.2b--first-hires.md).
  <!-- sources: src-065 (practitioner contribution) -->

- **At Growth+, the same if/then discipline scales with a dedicated finance team and board cycle** — quarterly reforecast, department owners, board packs.
  Learn the habit at 10 people first; a slick deck without trigger rules is still calendar fiction in PowerPoint.
  <!-- sources: src-065 (practitioner contribution) -->

## Context variants

### If you are bootstrapped

Trigger gates matter more — every hire must pass a runway floor after fully loaded cost. Review triggers weekly if cash is tight; the downside rows are not optional.

### If you have angel or friends & family investors

Keep the internal trigger list as the source of truth. A short scenario section in your monthly update mirrors it — do not maintain a separate "investor version" that diverges from what you actually execute.

### If you are venture-backed (seed / Series A)

Align the trigger list with your lead investor or board before you need it — especially downside rows and fundraise-prep triggers. They do not need three scenarios; they need to know what you will do when a line crosses.

### If you are venture-backed (growth / PE)

Not typical at first hires — if you are already here, the same one-page trigger discipline applies until a finance hire owns the cycle.
