# Accounts Payable & Expense Management — Foundation

> At foundation, your expense policy should be a principle, not a rulebook. Trust and speed matter more than formal control.

## What good looks like at this stage

- **Your expense policy can be a simple principle: spend company money as carefully as your own, without requiring pre-approval for every purchase.** When the team is small, trust and speed matter more than formal control. A principle lets people buy what they need without turning founders or managers into approval bottlenecks. The policy works because the organization is small enough for context, mistakes, and edge cases to travel quickly through conversation. Keep an eye on how people spend, without judgement — different people have different spending habits and in good faith may be spending accordingly while that does not align with the idea of other founders.
  <!-- sources: src-021-gitlab (GitLab Team Handbook, 2015) -->

## What you actually need to do

1. **Give people a lightweight escalation norm: if they are unsure whether an expense is appropriate, inform their manager before or soon after the purchase.** This preserves speed for obvious purchases while creating a low-friction path for judgment calls. It is not a formal approval workflow yet; it is a backstop for ambiguity.
   <!-- sources: src-021-gitlab (GitLab Team Handbook, 2015) -->

## Warning signs you're behind

### Output quality

- **Expense reports are consistently late or missing, or personal charges on company cards sit unresolved for weeks.** The lightweight policy only works when the basic hygiene still happens.
  <!-- sources: src-021-gitlab (GitLab Team Handbook, 2015) | flags: unverified -->

## How this evolves next

At First Hires (~5–25 people), a principle-based policy usually needs to become a written policy with clearer categories: what is reimbursable, what needs pre-approval, and what expense reporting tool to use. The escalation norm becomes a formal approval workflow for larger purchases.

## Tools & resources

- **For a remote-first early team, focus reimbursement on enabling work: laptop, peripherals, headset, desk, chair, internet, phone, and office space if home is impractical.** At this phase, the useful policy question is not procurement optimization; it is whether people have the basic setup needed to work effectively.
