# Company-Level Planning & Goals — First Hires

> At First Hires, planning is not a system — it's a habit. The goal is shared understanding of what matters most right now, reviewed often enough that it actually drives decisions.

## What good looks like at this stage

- **Everyone can name the top 3 priorities without looking anything up.**
  Goals live in people's heads and in conversation — a document alone doesn't make them deeply entrenched enough. If you ask anyone on the team what matters most this quarter, they should answer immediately.
  <!-- sources: src-010 (firstround_review) -->

- **You have one primary growth metric that drives weekly decisions.**
  You know it, your team knows it, and you use it as a filter. Next to that growth metric, you'll have 2-6 supporting metrics that together determine whether the business is healthy.
  <!-- sources: src-012 (paulgraham) -->
  <!-- sources: human:aksie | flags: unverified -->

- **Goals are ambitious enough that 70% is a good result.**
  If you're hitting everything, you're not setting real goals. Be aware: in Dutch culture, not reaching a goal is seen as very bad — there's a tendency to set safely achievable goals. If your team has that background, calibrate explicitly.
  <!-- sources: src-010 (firstround_review) -->
  <!-- sources: human:aksie | flags: unverified, geographically_biased -->

## What you actually need to do

- **Write down 3 company goals for the quarter with measurable results.**
  Not tasks, but outcomes you can score. Keep it to one page.
  <!-- sources: src-013 (gitlab_handbook, gitlab_scale_bias) -->

- **Share your thinking before asking the team to propose goals.**
  Tell them what you know, what you're uncertain about, and what the company absolutely needs to get right. Then ask for input. Without context, you get 15 disconnected proposals.
  <!-- sources: src-011 (firstround_review, series_a_plus_bias) -->

- **Review progress every week — not just every quarter.**
  In your team meeting: what progress did we make, what's blocking us, where do we need help? Bi-weekly works too, but less than that takes the edge off.
  <!-- sources: src-010 (firstround_review) -->
  <!-- sources: human:aksie | flags: unverified -->

- **Run a short retrospective at the start of each quarter.**
  How did we do against last quarter's goals? Why? What do we do differently? Then set new ones.
  <!-- sources: src-010 (firstround_review) -->

## Warning signs you're behind

### Output quality

- **Nobody can name the top 3 priorities without checking a document.**
  If the goals aren't in people's heads, they're not driving decisions.
  <!-- sources: src-010 (firstround_review) -->

- **Goals exist in a document written at quarter start and not looked at since.**
  A goal that isn't reviewed regularly is a wish, not a goal.
  <!-- sources: src-010 (firstround_review) -->

### Process entry

- **Team proposed 15 disconnected goals because you gave no context first.**
  Goal-setting without shared context produces noise. The fix is to share your thinking before asking for input.
  <!-- sources: src-011 (firstround_review, series_a_plus_bias) -->

- **You're asking individual contributors to set strategic goals they weren't hired to set.**
  Your first hires were hired for functional excellence — the best designer, the best engineer — not for translating strategy into tactics. If you delegate goal-setting before that capability exists, you get bad goals or you end up correcting everything yourself.
  <!-- sources: human:aksie | flags: unverified -->

## How this evolves next

- **At Early Scale (~25 people), informal goal-setting breaks down.**
  Multiple teams form with different focuses. You need a structured planning process with clear roles: who shares context, who proposes plans, who integrates and decides. With one team the founder can hold it in their head — with multiple teams, you need explicit roles or you get top-down dictation or bottom-up chaos.
  <!-- sources: src-011 (firstround_review, series_a_plus_bias) -->

## Tools & resources

- **Framework choice:** Consider OGSM (Objectives, Goals, Strategies, Measures) over OKR for a team of 2-10. OKR is process-heavy and light on strategy — OGSM forces you to connect the objective to a strategy before jumping to metrics.
  <!-- sources: human:aksie | flags: unverified -->
- A shared Google Doc or Notion page with this quarter's 3-5 goals and key results — no dedicated tool needed yet
- Weekly (or bi-weekly) team meeting with standing agenda: progress against goals, blockers, where we need help
- Quarterly all-hands devoted to reviewing results and setting new goals together
