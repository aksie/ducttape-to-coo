# Internal Communication about Strategy — Growth (~50–150 people)

> Information cascades up to leadership long before it reliably cascades down. At growth stage, repeating the strategy story is operational work — not a side project after planning.

## What good looks like at this stage

- **Reports flow up; the story must flow back down on the same schedule as planning.**
  Leadership can align in the management team every week — that does not mean teams heard what changed. After each planning cycle, pass strategy, priorities, and context down to the people doing the work.
  <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner) -->

- **Founders and executives expect to repeat the same strategy story far more often than feels natural.**
  In a company of roughly fifty people, each person may hear it once a year while the leader feels they repeat it weekly — that gap is under-communication for the organisation, not over-communication by the leader. Scale the example to your headcount.

## Warning signs you're behind

### Output quality

- **The management team knows the plan; the rest of the company often doesn't.**
  If your investor deck is current but teams are still executing against a plan from six months ago, the problem isn't alignment at the top — it's that nobody is telling people below what changed.
  <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner) -->

- **Strategy lives in the founder's head and the investor deck while the organisation runs on an outdated plan.**
  Deck updates without operational cascade produce silent misalignment across functions.
  <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner) -->

### Process entry

- **Nobody owns downward transmission on the planning rhythm — so the planning job is not finished.**
  Treating internal comms as separate from strategy (instead of a direct derivative of it) leaves a gap that shows up as teams executing on context the leadership assumed was shared.
