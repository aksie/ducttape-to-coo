# Company-Level Planning & Goals — Growth (~50–150 people)

> At growth stage, planning becomes an operating rhythm across multiple time horizons — not just a quarterly goal list.

## What good looks like at this stage

- **Planning operates as a cadence system across time horizons: week, month, quarter, year, strategy, vision, and mission.**
  Growth adds recurring planning events that cannot be coordinated from a single quarterly goal list. Cadence turns planning into an operating rhythm.
  <!-- sources: src-024 (GitLab) -->

- **Running metrics (company top line) are set from C-level with feasibility input; change goals are owned locally.**
  Pick metrics that fit your business — practitioners often use MRR, NPS, or eNPS at the top line. Each department owns three to five real problems or chances at year horizon, then quarter — complementing top-line metrics with owned local improvement agendas.
  <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner) | flags: conditional -->

- **Departments pre-book only ~70% of capacity on their own objectives** — reserving slack for cross-team dependencies (marketing ↔ engineering ↔ legal).
  When two departments collide, C-level resolves priority. The ~70% figure is a practitioner rule of thumb, not a universal benchmark.
  <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner) | flags: conditional, needs practitioner check -->

## What you actually need to do

1. **Before changing planning rhythm, name who owns the process and who maintains it.**
  A process is an efficiency move to get relevant information to decision-makers — changing frameworks without ownership usually reverts within a quarter.
  <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner) | flags: needs practitioner check -->

## Warning signs you're behind

### Output quality

- **Strategic debate lands in a weekly progress meeting** — unprepared room, rushed decisions, bad outcomes.
  Wrong meeting layer wastes executive time and forces choices without context or data.
  <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner) -->

- **Departments run at 100% local load on their own objectives** — permanent gridlock on shared dependencies.
  Cross-team work needs unbooked capacity; fully booked departmental plans cannot absorb interdependencies.
  <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner) -->

### Founder / key-person time

- **Around eighty people, every department plans differently — and executives keep re-deciding things the CPO, CRO, or other domain head should own.**
  That usually means planning was never clearly owned below the executive team. Headcount will vary — the pattern is uneven planning plus executive override, not a magic number.
  <!-- sources: src-064 (anonymous-practitioner) | flags: conditional -->

### Process entry

- **The company only does planning once per quarter and everything else is ad hoc.**
  Without a cadence, important planning events (strategy review, vision check, annual planning) get squeezed out by the urgency of the quarter.
  <!-- sources: src-024 (GitLab) -->

## How this evolves next

- **OKR style goals need to sit inside a broader company cadence with board meetings, sales targets, metrics reviews, yearly planning, strategy, vision, and mission.**
  OKRs coordinate goals, but they do not coordinate every recurring management process. A growing company needs both the goal system and the operating calendar to fit together.
