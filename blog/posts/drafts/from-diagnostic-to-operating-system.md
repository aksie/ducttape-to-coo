Most startup "operations advice" is either too vague or too late.

You get generic playbooks, or you get a data-room panic two weeks before a fundraise. What's missing is something in between: **structured, stage-specific guidance** you can actually run the company on — and improve over time.

That's what I've been building.

## It usually starts with the same question

A founder or CEO reaches out: *"I think we need a COO. Can you help us? Or do you know someone?"*

My answer is: probably not yet — and you may not actually need one full-time. Before we dive into solutions, let's figure out what your actual problem is. I *can* help you with that. Let's go through your ops.

That conversation is what **Duct Tape to COO** is for.

## It started with a diagnostic

Last year I helped a handful of founders in exactly these situations. At first I improvised my analysis. After a few cases, I noticed I was doing roughly the same thing each time — and with some long conversations I worked out what the method actually was. I turned it into a structured questionnaire. Ruben then challenged me to make it into a web app. And there was the diagnostic framework.

The first piece is simple and practical: a **diagnostics framework** — a structured questionnaire that adapts to where the company actually is (foundation, first hires, early revenue, growth, scaled).

We've run it with several startups. It doesn't hand you a maturity score to frame on the wall. It gives you a **prioritized to-do list**: what to fix *now*, in the context of *your* stage.

Most ops frameworks tell you what good looks like in general. This one tells you what good looks like *at your specific stage*, and where you are right now relative to that. Which processes apply depends on what stage you're at as a company:

- Three founders and a dog? You need almost nothing.
- 35 people, paying customers, investors on the cap table? You need a real operational backbone.

For each process we look at how broken it is right now, who owns it (and whether it has a clear owner), whether it's documented, what tooling supports it, and how much extra scale it can handle.

That worked. Then someone asked the obvious next question: *"Can this live inside my AI setup — as a skill that knows our stage and our processes?"*

That question changed the roadmap.

## From static tool to living knowledge

To power an AI skill, you need more than a form. You need a **knowledge base** — processes, warning signs, templates, and the reasoning behind them.

So we're building a **wiki**: process × stage guidance covering strategic, financial, people, legal, and revenue ops. Some pages are written directly from operator experience — including transaction-readiness filing for legal, finance, and HR. Others come through a **content pipeline**: find sources → extract claims → synthesize drafts → **human review in a browser tool** → publish only what's approved.

The pipeline isn't "AI replaces judgment." It's **AI does the grunt work; humans keep the accountability.**

## Templates and blog — the layers around the wiki

While filling the wiki, two other needs kept showing up:

1. **Depth on recurring themes** — why strategy breaks when founders don't cascade it, or why "transaction readiness" isn't a folder you create the week before due diligence. That's **blog** territory: fewer pages, more narrative.

2. **Repeatable operational artifacts** — a "Working at" hub, quarterly goals layout, due diligence checklist, **Company Ops Registry** (where contracts, tax, HR, and compliance actually live). Not Notion pages locked in someone's account — **Markdown templates** that render as clean HTML for the team and stay **editable by agents** ("add the Berlin office to the handbook," "file this contract under HR").

That's a deliberate architecture choice: **Markdown in git, HTML for humans, agents for maintenance.** Open, forkable, no vendor lock-in.

## Why this exists

My ambition is to make this kind of operational clarity available to more founders and organisations than I could ever personally help — through the framework being open and free, through other ops people picking it up and using it with their own clients, and increasingly with AI doing more of the heavy lifting.

A lot of my work is pro bono with non-profits and mission-driven organisations who can't easily afford a fractional COO but need this thinking just as much. The framework being open is what makes that scalable. I keep doing some commercial work too — partly to fund that work, partly because scale-ups and growth-stage startups are what keeps the framework honest in those settings. The two feed each other.

## Where this is going

The north star is an **open-source, AI-driven fractional COO**:

- For founders and small teams: guidance and execution support without hiring a full ops leader on day one.
- For COOs, Chiefs of Staff, and ops leads: a **supercharged playbook** — diagnostic + wiki + templates + skills — that stays current and scales with the company.

We're not claiming the wiki is "done." It's a living system: more stages, more processes, more templates, tighter links between diagnostic results and the exact wiki page you need next.

But the direction is clear: **less duct tape in founders' heads, more operational clarity in the open** — for humans and for the agents that will increasingly work alongside them.

Try it, use it — it's open source. You can self-diagnose, but it works better with an experienced ops person walking you through it. They'll know what "good" looks like for your stage, and can review your actual processes. Ask someone in your network, or reach out — we'll figure out whether you need a COO, or just AI-augmented duct tape.
