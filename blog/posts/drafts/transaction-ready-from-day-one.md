## The week I realised I wasn't as prepared as I thought

When we were preparing for a fundraising round, I approached it the way I'd approached due diligence from the buy side: first impressions in a data room matter more than founders realise. A disorganised data room signals broader organisational problems. It makes investors more critical of everything that follows.

So I made sure the structure looked clean and considered for the stage we were at. I was confident.

Then I went through everything with our external legal advisor.

Missing addenda confirming salary changes. Contracts that hadn't actually been signed. Documents filed inconsistently across three different folders. It was a solid week of work to get things properly in order — and that was with someone who had done M&A due diligence professionally and knew exactly what to look for.

I was glad we caught it ourselves first. Had the investors' legal team found those gaps, it would have damaged our negotiating position — not because the underlying issues were serious, but because disorganisation signals risk. The likely outcome would have been either a lower price or a longer list of reps and warranties.

The cost of that week of preparation was a fraction of what it would have cost us at the table.

---

## Why this keeps happening

Most founders only think about their legal and financial filing when a transaction is imminent: a fundraising round, an acquisition approach, a new investor doing a re-up. By then, you're also in the most time-pressured period — and the cost of gaps is highest.

The structural problem is that there's no natural forcing function for getting organised earlier. Annual accounts get filed because there's a legal deadline. Contracts get signed because the deal won't close without them. But filing them somewhere findable, with a one-liner about what they cover? That's the kind of thing that slips to "later."

Later turns out to be a week before you open the data room.

## What actually needs to be maintained — and from when

The good news: most of this takes very little ongoing effort if you set it up right from the start. The cost is a few hours of setup and a few minutes per document as things happen.

Here's how it maps to company stages:

**From day one:**
- Deed of incorporation, articles of association, shareholder register
- Cap table — even if it's a one-tab spreadsheet
- Any co-founder agreements or vesting agreements
- Trademark and domain registrations (with renewal dates noted)

**From first hire:**
- Employment contracts, filed in a known location — every one, not just the key people
- Freelance and contractor agreements with explicit IP assignment clauses
- Standard templates for both — reviewed by a lawyer at least once

**From early revenue:**
- Annual accounts, signed and filed within weeks of the AGM — not "somewhere in email"
- Tax filings — corporate, VAT, payroll — each in its own folder
- A one-page document that bridges your management financials to your statutory accounts (more on this below)
- Insurance policies with renewal dates
- A simple compliance calendar: what needs to be filed or renewed, and when

---

## The one document most companies don't have (and really should)

The management-to-statutory bridge is consistently the most time-consuming item to reconstruct in a due diligence process. It's also the easiest to maintain year-round.

Every company has two versions of its financials: the statutory annual accounts (what's filed with the tax authority and chamber of commerce) and the management accounts (how you actually track the business month-to-month). These differ — sometimes significantly — for legitimate reasons: revenue recognition timing, EBITDA adjustments, non-recurring items, deferred revenue.

Buyers and investors expect these differences. What they don't expect is that you can't explain them.

A single page — or a single tab in a spreadsheet — that says "here's why these two numbers differ, and here's the reasoning behind each adjustment" is worth more in a due diligence process than almost any other document. Write it once a year when you prepare the annual accounts, update it as you go, and file it next to the annual accounts.

## The directory structure that makes this easy to maintain

The goal is a filing structure where any document has exactly one obvious home. When something new comes in, there's no decision to make — it goes in its place. When something needs to be found, there's no searching.

Here's a structure that works well for companies from first hire through Series A:

```
/Legal & Finance/
├── 01-corporate/
│   ├── incorporation/
│   ├── shareholders/         ← SHA, cap table, register
│   └── board/                ← minutes, D&O, charter
├── 02-finance/
│   ├── annual-accounts/
│   ├── tax/                  ← VPB, BTW, payroll, WBSO
│   ├── management-reporting/
│   └── bank-and-signatories/
├── 03-contracts/
│   ├── customers/
│   ├── suppliers/
│   └── ndas/
├── 04-ip/
│   └── trademarks-and-domains/   ← include renewal dates
├── 05-insurance/
├── 06-hr/
│   ├── employment-contracts/
│   ├── freelance-contracts/
│   └── templates/
├── 07-compliance/
│   ├── gdpr/
│   └── grants-and-subsidies/
└── 08-transaction-prep/          ← only when a deal is active
```

The `08-transaction-prep` folder is deliberately empty year-round. When a deal is active, you populate it with the transaction-specific analysis (financial deep-dives, tech DD, commercial analysis). But the fact that `01` through `07` are current means this can be done quickly, not in a panic.

---

## The external review is worth it

One more thing from that week of preparation: going through it with an external legal advisor was the most valuable part.

Not because I didn't know what I was looking for — I did. But because it's very hard to see the gaps in your own filing. You know the context. You know why a particular document is "fine even though it's not signed" or why that addendum "doesn't really matter." An advisor sees it the way a buyer's legal team will see it: as a signal.

Budget a few hours with your legal advisor once a year to walk through the structure. The cost is small. The benefit — knowing that a buyer or investor will see an organised, professional data room — is significant.
